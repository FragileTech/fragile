## 0) Set‑up and minimal hypotheses

Let $G$ be a compact Lie group (e.g. $SU(N)$). On a hypercubic Euclidean lattice with spacing $a$, links carry variables $U_\ell\in G$. For a plaquette $p$, $U_p$ is the ordered product of links around $p$. Your dynamics induce a *local, gauge‑invariant* Boltzmann factor $K_a: G\to\mathbb{R}_+$ and a finite‑range action that is a sum of class functions of Wilson loops (plaquettes, rectangles, …). We will focus first on a single‑plaquette factorized measure

$$
\mathrm{d}\mu_a(U)\;\propto\;\prod_{p} K_a(U_p)\, \mathrm{d}U,
$$

and then discuss robustness to multi‑loop terms.

**Hypothesis (PD).** For each $a$, $K_a$ is a **central positive‑definite (PD)** function on $G$.

* For compact groups, central PD $\iff$ **nonnegative character expansion**:

  $$
  K_a(g)=\sum_{R\in \widehat G} c_R(a)\,\chi_R(g),\qquad c_R(a)\ge 0,\quad c_{\text{triv}}(a)>0,
  $$

  where $\chi_R$ are irreducible characters. This is the Bochner–Godement/Peter–Weyl picture specialized to class functions. (For an explicit example: the **heat kernel** on $G$ has expansion $p_t(g)=\sum_R d_R\,e^{-t C_2(R)}\chi_R(g)$ with all coefficients positive.) ([arXiv][4])

> **How you can *verify (PD)* in your framework.** If your induced plaquette weight is (or stochastically dominates) a **subordinated heat kernel** on $G$ (mixtures of $p_t$ with $t\ge 0$), then all $c_R(a)\ge 0$ automatically; products/convolutions of central PD kernels remain PD, so any finite superposition generated by your dynamics preserves (PD). These are standard closure properties for PD kernels and for Lévy/heat semigroups on compact Lie groups. ([SpringerLink][5], [ResearchGate][6])

## 1) Reflection positivity (RP) and the transfer matrix

**Theorem 1 (RP from PD plaquette kernels).**
Consider the factorized lattice gauge measure $\prod_p K_a(U_p)$ with each $K_a$ central PD. Then the theory is **reflection positive** with respect to any lattice reflection plane orthogonal to a coordinate axis. Consequently, the RP bilinear form $\langle \Theta F, F\rangle\ge 0$ for all gauge‑invariant cylinder functionals $F$, and a **positive self‑adjoint transfer matrix** exists (after the standard temporal gauge fixing).

*Sketch.* Expand each $K_a$ into characters with $c_R(a)\ge 0$. Writing the partition function as a contraction of representation tensors across the reflection hyperplane, the cross‑term becomes a sum of squared norms with nonnegative weights $c_R(a)$, hence RP. This is precisely the strategy of the general RP framework for lattice models; for the Wilson action this is classical (Osterwalder–Seiler; Lüscher’s construction of a positive transfer matrix). The only extra ingredient here is $c_R(a)\ge 0$, guaranteed by (PD). ([ADS][2], [OSTI][7], [SpringerLink][8])

> **Robustness to multi‑loop terms.** RP is stable under multiplication by factors whose character coefficients remain nonnegative; i.e., if the action is a *sum of local terms whose Boltzmann weights are central PD*, RP still holds (use that products of PD functions are PD and repeat the tensor‑contraction argument). In contrast, certain “improved” gauge actions with *indefinite* Fourier/character components can break RP; in fact, for general positivity one essentially falls back to the **Wilson structure** (or PD‑equivalent variants). ([Wikipedia][9], [iris.uniroma1.it][10])

**Corollary 1 (Transfer matrix).** Under Theorem 1’s hypotheses, there exists a **strictly positive, self‑adjoint transfer matrix** generating a unitary real‑time evolution after OS reconstruction. This is textbook for the Wilson action (and extends under the same PD mechanism). ([OSTI][3])

**Remark (gluon fields vs physical spectrum).** Reflection positivity applies to gauge‑invariant operators. Elitzur’s theorem forbids spontaneous breaking of local gauge symmetry, so states must be created by gauge‑invariant functionals (Wilson loops, glueball operators), dovetailing with the transfer‑matrix spectral analysis. ([Wikipedia][11])

## 2) Strong‑coupling area law and confinement bound

In the strong‑coupling regime (small $\beta$ or, in a general PD description, when the weight of nontrivial representations is sufficiently suppressed vs. the trivial one), the character expansion gives a direct combinatorial control of Wilson loops.

**Theorem 2 (General Wilson confinement bound via PD coefficients).**
Let $K_a(g)=\sum_R c_R(a)\chi_R(g)$ with $c_R(a)\ge 0$. Fix a faithful representation $F$ used to define Wilson loops. If

$$
\eta(a)\;\equiv\;\sup_{R\neq \text{triv}} \frac{c_R(a)}{c_{\text{triv}}(a)}\;<\;\eta_0(d,G)\ll 1,
$$

then there exists $\sigma(a)>0$ and $C(a)$ such that for any rectangular loop $C$,

$$
\langle W_F(C)\rangle\;\le\;C(a)\,\exp\!\big(-\sigma(a)\,\mathrm{Area}(C)\big),
$$

with $\sigma(a)\asymp -\log \eta(a)$ as $\eta(a)\downarrow 0$.

*Sketch.* Insert the loop into the character expansion, use orthogonality to tile with minimal surfaces, and bound the sum by $\eta(a)^{\#\text{plaquettes}}$. This is the standard strong‑coupling/character‑expansion area‑law argument that underlies Wilson’s original mechanism and its rigorous versions (cluster/chessboard estimates). ([APS Journals][12], [ADS][2], [OSTI][13], [SpringerLink][14])

**Notes.**

* (i) The constant $\eta_0(d,G)$ depends on coordination and representation theory but can be made explicit with standard cluster‑expansion controls.
* (ii) The same method provides perimeter‑law bounds above/deep into the crossover when $\eta(a)$ is not small.
* (iii) For thorough physics background, see Kogut’s RMP review; for mathematically rigorous treatments at strong coupling, see Osterwalder–Seiler (existence/analyticity of the infinite‑volume limit and Wilson’s bound) and work on cluster expansions in lattice gauge theory. ([APS Journals][15], [ADS][2], [OSTI][13])

## 3) Mass gap at strong coupling

**Theorem 3 (Exponential decay of gauge‑invariant correlators ⇒ mass gap).**
Under Theorem 1 and the smallness condition in Theorem 2, connected two‑point functions of localized gauge‑invariant operators decay as

$$
\big|\langle \mathcal{O}(x)\mathcal{O}(0)\rangle_c\big|\;\le\;A(a)\,e^{-m(a)\,|x|},\qquad m(a)>0.
$$

Equivalently, the transfer‑matrix Hamiltonian has a **spectral gap** above the vacuum in the gauge‑invariant sector.

*Sketch.* Area‑law control for appropriate loop or glueball operators gives exponential fall‑off in Euclidean time separations; RP translates this into positivity of the transfer matrix and hence a spectral gap. This route is standard (see OS‑positivity → transfer matrix → spectral representation; lattice strong‑coupling bounds turn the decay into an explicit mass scale). ([SpringerLink][1], [ADS][2])

## 4) Continuum OS limit: tightness + reconstruction under functional‑inequality control

Beyond strong coupling (fixed $a$), one must control families of measures $\{\mu_a\}$ as $a\downarrow 0$.

**Hypothesis (Uniform functional inequalities).** There is a coarse‑graining (block‑spin) scheme such that each $\mu_a$ satisfies a **log‑Sobolev inequality** (LSI) with constant bounded below uniformly along the RG trajectory in a neighborhood of the target scaling regime, and the perturbations generated by block integration obey a Holley–Stroock type bounded‑oscillation condition.

These conditions are standard sufficient criteria for:

* **Exponential mixing** (spectral gap) and **cluster properties**, hence tightness of Schwinger functions;
* Stability of LSI under bounded perturbations/coarse‑graining;
* Construction of infinite‑volume limits and OS‑reconstruction of a Wightman QFT from Euclidean data. ([PMC][16], [SpringerLink][17])

**Theorem 4 (Tightness and OS reconstruction under LSI control).**
Assuming RP (Theorem 1) and Hypothesis (Uniform functional inequalities), the Schwinger functions at lattice spacing $a$ form a tight family; any weak limit along $a_k\downarrow 0$ yields Euclidean $n$‑point functions satisfying the OS axioms (including RP). By the OS theorem, these analytically continue to Wightman distributions of a (possibly effective) quantum gauge theory. ([SpringerLink][1])

> **Why this is realistic here.** On compact manifolds/groups one has baseline LSI (Bakry–Émery) for heat‑kernel measures; Holley–Stroock perturbation lemmas keep LSI under bounded‑oscillation local interactions; Stroock–Zegarlinski show equivalence to mixing criteria used in cluster expansions. These are the standard functional‑analytic handles needed for tightness and continuum reconstruction. ([PMC][16], [SpringerLink][18])

The defense presented here shows a *rock‑solid path* in the **regime** where your induced weights satisfy (PD) with $\eta(a)$ small (strong‑coupling side) and outlines the uniform‑LSI conditions needed for a continuum OS limit. ## 7) Formal statements to include verbatim

> **Definition 1 (Central PD).** A continuous class function $K:G\to\mathbb{C}$ is *positive‑definite* if $\sum_{i,j}\overline{\alpha_i}\alpha_j K(g_i^{-1}g_j)\ge 0$ for all finite $\{\alpha_i,g_i\}$. For compact $G$, $K$ admits a character expansion $K=\sum_{R}c_R\chi_R$ with $c_R\ge 0$ iff $K$ is central PD. ([Wikipedia][9], [arXiv][4])

> **Theorem A (RP for PD lattice gauge weights).**
> Let $K_a$ be central PD. Then the lattice gauge theory with weight $\prod_p K_a(U_p)$ is reflection positive; hence a positive self‑adjoint transfer matrix exists. (Proof via character expansion and the general RP framework.) ([ADS][2], [OSTI][3])

> **Theorem B (Wilson bound from coefficients).**
> With $K_a=\sum_R c_R(a)\chi_R$ and $\eta(a)=\sup_{R\neq 1} c_R/c_1$, there is $\eta_0(d,G)>0$ such that if $\eta(a)<\eta_0$ then
> $\langle W_F(C)\rangle \le C(a) e^{-\sigma(a)\mathrm{Area}(C)}$ with $\sigma(a)\gtrsim -\log\eta(a)$. ([APS Journals][12], [ADS][2])

> **Theorem C (Mass gap at strong coupling).**
> Under Theorem A and the smallness condition in Theorem B, connected gauge‑invariant correlators decay exponentially; the transfer‑matrix Hamiltonian has a spectral gap $m(a)>0$. ([SpringerLink][1])

> **Theorem D (Continuum OS reconstruction under uniform LSI).**
> If $\{\mu_a\}$ satisfy reflection positivity and a uniform LSI/mixing bound stable under coarse‑graining, the Schwinger functions are tight; any scaling limit satisfies the OS axioms and reconstructs a Wightman theory. ([PMC][16], [SpringerLink][17])
