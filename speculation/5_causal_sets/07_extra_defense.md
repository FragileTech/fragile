## 1) SDEs on bundles and generators: well‑posedness and smoothing

**Theorem 1 (Existence, uniqueness, and geometric consistency).**
Let $X_t$ be the solution of a Stratonovich SDE on a smooth bundle $\mathcal B\to M$ (e.g., $UM$ with the Sasaki metric) with smooth coefficients satisfying linear growth/Lipschitz bounds. Then:

1. A unique strong solution exists up to explosion time; on compact $\mathcal B$ there is no explosion.
2. The process projects to a diffusion on $M$ whose generator is (a multiple of) the Laplace–Beltrami/hypoelliptic operator dictated by the chosen geometry (e.g., the horizontal lift on $O(M)$ yields Brownian motion).
3. The heat kernel is smooth for $t>0$; if the Hörmander bracket condition holds (kinetic/FP case), the transition densities are $C^\infty$.

*Why this matters.* This answers “the Sasaki/UTB dynamics are non‑standard” by pinning them to canonical constructions.
*Sources.* Hsu’s monograph (SDEs on manifolds, frame‑bundle construction), Hörmander’s theorem, recent surveys on Kolmogorov equations. ([Google Books][1], [sayanmuk.github.io][2], [arXiv][4])

**Theorem 2 (Hörmander hypoellipticity for kinetic FP).**
For kinetic FP/kolmogorov operators $ \partial_t f + v\!\cdot\!\nabla_x f = \nabla_v\!\cdot(\nabla_v f + (\nabla U)v f)$ (and perturbations), the Lie algebra generated by the velocity diffusion and transport spans the tangent space, so the operator is hypoelliptic; solutions become instantly smooth. ([arXiv][4])

**Theorem 3 (Quantitative decay by hypocoercivity / Harris).**
Under mild confinement (e.g., quadratic‑like at infinity) or on compact bundles, the semigroup is exponentially convergent to equilibrium in $L^2$ (hypocoercivity) and in Wasserstein/total variation (via Harris drift+minorization). Robust variants yield algebraic rates for subquadratic potentials. ([arXiv][14], [ResearchGate][15], [aimsciences.org][16])

**Remark (Nonconvex landscapes).**
Even for nonconvex $U$, reflection couplings yield explicit contraction in $W_1/W_2$ when $U$ is strictly dissipative outside a ball. This neutralizes “your Lyapunov only works in convex regimes” critiques. ([online.kitp.ucsb.edu][17])

## 2) Killing (“death penalty”), FK semigroups, Doob normalization

**Theorem 4 (Feynman–Kac with killing is positivity‑preserving; normalization yields a genuine Markov dynamics).**
Let $P_t$ be the Markov semigroup for the baseline diffusion and $V\ge 0$ a measurable potential. Define the FK semigroup $(T_t f)(x)=\mathbb E_x\!\left[e^{-\int_0^t V(X_s)\,ds} f(X_t)\right]$. Then:

* $T_t$ is positivity preserving and strongly continuous.
* If $\lambda_0$ is the principal eigenvalue of $L-V$ (under suitable boundary conditions), the *Doob $h$-transform* $ \widetilde T_t f = e^{\lambda_0 t} h^{-1} T_t(h f)$ is Markov and corresponds to conditioning on non‑absorption (“$Q$-process”); it leaves the quasi‑stationary distribution (QSD) invariant and is exponentially mixing under standard drift/minorization. ([SIAM Ebooks][6], [Elsevier Shop][18], [arXiv][19])

*Consequence.* The “death‑penalty” term is not ad hoc; it is the canonical potential killing in FK theory and its normalization is a textbook Doob transform. This keeps reflection/positivity properties intact when $V$ is local.

**Theorem 5 (QSD existence & exponential convergence).**
For diffusions with killing satisfying a Lyapunov drift and a minorization on a petite set (or one‑dimensional natural boundary settings), a QSD exists and the conditioned law converges exponentially fast to it; the associated $Q$-process is ergodic. ([alea.impa.br][20], [SpringerLink][21])

**OS‑positivity check (Euclidean field‑theory consistency).**
Adding a local nonnegative potential weight $e^{-\int V}$ to a reflection‑symmetric Gaussian measure preserves Osterwalder–Schrader reflection positivity; the reconstruction to a unitary QFT is unaffected. This addresses “not physical”—the FK/death‑penalty weight is compatible with OS‑axioms. ([ScienceDirect][22])

## 3) Functional inequalities and gradient flows (KL/TV/Wasserstein/FR/WFR)

**Theorem 6 (Entropy dissipation ⇒ convergence; AGS calculus + JKO).**
On a compact manifold (or with confinement), the Fokker–Planck equation is the $W_2$-gradient flow of $H(\cdot|\pi)$. If $\pi$ satisfies a Poincaré/LSI (e.g., by Bakry–Émery curvature or compactness), then $H(\mu_t|\pi)$ decays exponentially; Csiszár–Pinsker then yields TV decay. **Theorem 7 (Fisher–Rao uniqueness & legitimacy of IG).**
The Fisher–Rao metric is (up to scale) the unique information metric contracting under Markov morphisms (Čencov/Amari). Using FR on densities is *the* canonical choice—one is not “breaking rules” by working in Information Geometry. ([ism.ac.jp][24], [SpringerLink][25])

**Theorem 8 (Wasserstein–Fisher–Rao = Hellinger–Kantorovich; dynamics with creation/annihilation).**
HK/WFR is a bona‑fide geodesic distance obtained as the dynamic Benamou–Brenier energy with a reaction term, and also via a static entropy–transport formulation; it metrizes transport with mass change and admits convexity/gradient‑flow structures. This justifies mixing FR and $W_2$ in your Lyapunov tools. ([arXiv][26], [ScienceDirect][27])

## 4) Spectral and geometric controls on compact bases

**Theorem 9 (Discrete spectrum & Poincaré).**
On a compact Riemannian manifold, $-\Delta_g$ has a discrete spectrum $0=\lambda_0<\lambda_1\le\lambda_2\le\cdots\to\infty$ and the Poincaré inequality $\|f-\bar f\|_2^2\le \lambda_1^{-1}\|\nabla f\|_2^2$ holds. Hence any diffusion with a smooth reversible density satisfies spectral‑gap based exponential decay. ([math.mcgill.ca][28], [Google Books][29])

**Lemma (Curvature–dimension ⇒ LSI/heat‑kernel bounds).**
Under a Bakry–Émery $CD(\kappa,\infty)$ condition, one obtains LSI, parabolic Harnack, and ultracontractivity—directly feeding hypocoercive decay and concentration estimates. ([SpringerLink][30], [math.univ-toulouse.fr][31])

## 5) Bit threads, anisotropy, and continuum max‑flow/min‑cut

**Theorem 10 (Anisotropic continuous MFMC).**
Max‑flow/min‑cut holds in anisotropic continuous media: for a bounded Lipschitz domain $\Omega\subset\mathbb R^n$ with a positive definite conductivity/capacity tensor $\sigma(x)$, the maximum flux of divergence‑free fields bounded by $|\sigma^{1/2}v|\le 1$ equals the minimum anisotropic surface capacity. This subsumes direction‑dependent “thread” norms. ([SciSpace][13])

**Corollary (Compatibility with bit‑threads and covariant generalizations).**
The Freedman–Headrick bit‑thread formalism is a Riemannian MFMC duality; Headrick–Hubeny extend it to covariant $V$‑ and $U$-flows. Both frameworks tolerate spatially varying and anisotropic local bounds (via convex optimization duality). This undercuts “your anisotropic threads are not allowed.” ([arXiv][32], [SpringerLink][33])

## 6) Discretization faithfulness: PPP sampling and graph/variational limits

**Theorem 11 (Poisson sprinklings: invariance and concentration).**
A homogeneous PPP with intensity $\lambda$ relative to the Riemannian volume is invariant under isometries; counts in disjoint sets are independent Poisson, with exponential tail bounds (Chernoff‑type). This justifies PPP as the neutral “ignorance prior” and supports sharp finite‑sample controls. ([Oxford University Press][11], [Google Books][12], [Wikipedia][34])

**Theorem 12 (Graph/BV $\Gamma$-limits and spectral consistency).**
For PPP (or i.i.d. uniform) point clouds with appropriate connectivity radii, graph cuts/TV and graph Laplacians $\Gamma$-converge to their continuum BV/perimeter and Laplace–Beltrami counterparts; consequently, discrete max‑flow/min‑cut and spectral quantities converge. This rebuts “your continuum claims break at the discrete level.” ([Project Euclid][35])
