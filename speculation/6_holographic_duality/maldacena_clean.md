### 1.1 The Algorithmic Substrate: Episodes, CST, and IG

We begin by defining the discrete building blocks of our universe, which are generated by a run of the Relativistic Gas algorithm on a background Lorentzian manifold $(\mathcal{M}, g_{\mu\nu})$.

:::{prf:definition} Walker, Episode, and Events
:label: def-walker-episode-holo

- **Walker state:** A walker is a tuple $w=(x^\mu,u^\mu,s)$, where $x^\mu\in\mathcal{M}$ is its 4-position, $u^\mu$ is its future-timelike 4-velocity, and $s\in\{0,1\}$ is its survival status.
- **Episode:** For a given walker, an **episode** $e$ is the worldline segment between its birth and death events, $\gamma_e=\{x^\mu(t)\,:\,t\in[t^{\mathrm{birth}}, t^{\mathrm{death}}]\}$. The set of all episodes in a run is denoted $\mathcal{E}$.
- **Events:** The **birth event** $E^{\mathrm{birth}}_e=(t^{\mathrm{birth}},x^\mu(t^{\mathrm{birth}}))$ and the **death event** $E^{\mathrm{death}}_e=(t^{\mathrm{death}},x^\mu(t^{\mathrm{death}}))$.
:::

From the log file of a complete run, which contains the full history of all episodes, we construct two distinct graphs.

:::{prf:definition} Causal Spacetime Tree (CST)
:label: def-cst-holo

The **Causal Spacetime Tree (CST)** is the directed graph $T=(\mathcal{E},\to)$ whose nodes are the episodes $\mathcal{E}$. A directed edge $e_i \to e_j$ exists if and only if the birth of episode $e_j$ was a direct causal consequence of the death of episode $e_i$ (e.g., through cloning or replacement). The transitive closure of this relation defines a partial order $\prec$. Each edge is weighted by the proper time of the parent episode, $\omega(e_i \to e_j) := \tau_i$.
:::

:::{prf:definition} Information Graph (IG)
:label: def-ig-holo

The **Information Graph (IG)** is the weighted, undirected graph $G=(\mathcal{E},\sim)$ whose nodes are the episodes $\mathcal{E}$. An edge $e_i \sim e_j$ exists if the two episodes are coupled by a direct interaction during their lifespans. Criteria for an edge include:
1.  **Temporal overlap:** Their worldlines are co-temporal.
2.  **Selection coupling:** They participate in the same selection or cloning event where the outcome depends jointly on their fitness values.
3.  **Field interaction:** They interact via the mean-field back-reaction or any other explicit force carrier.
Each edge carries a non-negative weight $w_{ij}$ quantifying the interaction strength.
:::

## 2. The Informational Area Law: An Equivalence Theorem for IG Cuts and CST Surfaces

Having defined the CST as our geometric substrate and the IG as our informational one, we now arrive at the central theorem of the framework's holographic principle. We will prove that these two independent structures are not independent at all; rather, the informational entropy of a region's boundary, measured on the IG, is directly proportional to the geometric area of that boundary, measured on the CST. This result is not assumed but is a provable consequence of the algorithm's dynamics in a stationary regime.

### 2.1 Independent Measures of Boundary Area and Entanglement Entropy

First, we formalize the two quantities to be related. Let $A$ be a collection of episodes representing a subregion of the emergent spacetime (e.g., all episodes on a spatial slice within a certain volume).

:::{prf:definition} CST Boundary Area
:label: def-cst-area-holo

An **antichain** $S \subset \mathcal{E}$ is a set of episodes, no two of which are causally related by $\prec$. An antichain **separates** $A$ if every past-directed causal chain starting in $A$ must intersect $S$. The **minimal CST separating antichain**, denoted $\gamma_A$, is a separating antichain with the minimum possible number of episodes. The **CST Area** of this boundary is then defined as:

$$
\mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{CST}}(\gamma_A) := a_0 \, |\gamma_A|
$$

where $a_0 > 0$ is a fixed calibration constant that converts a discrete node count into a physical area. This definition is purely geometric, relying only on the causal structure of the CST.
:::

:::{prf:definition} IG Entanglement Entropy
:label: def-ig-entropy-holo

An **IG cut** $\Gamma$ is a set of edges in the Information Graph whose removal disconnects the nodes in $A$ from the nodes in its complement, $A^c$. The **capacity** of the cut is the sum of its edge weights, $\mathrm{Cap}(\Gamma) := \sum_{e \in \Gamma} w_e$. The **minimal IG cut**, denoted $\Gamma_{\min}(A)$, is a cut with the minimum possible capacity. The **IG Entropy** of the region $A$ is defined as this minimal capacity:

$$
S_{IG}(A) := \mathrm{Cap}(\Gamma_{\min}(A))
$$

This definition is purely informational, relying only on the interaction structure and weights of the IG.
:::

These two definitions set up a profound duality.
- **Area** is a measure of **how many discrete geometric elements** are needed to form a boundary. It is an extensive, geometric quantity derived from the causal structure.
- **Entropy** is a measure of the **minimum information flow** across a boundary. It is an intensive, informational quantity derived from the interaction structure.

The following sections will prove a deep and unexpected equivalence between these two seemingly disparate concepts.

### 2.2 From Discrete Cuts to a Continuum Perimeter

To prove the equivalence, we must first translate the discrete IG cut problem into the language of continuum geometry. We achieve this by showing that, in the mean-field limit, the expected capacity of the minimal IG cut converges to a well-defined geometric functional known as a nonlocal perimeter.

Let the algorithm be in its stationary QSD regime, such that the mean-field density of episodes is given by a smooth, time-independent distribution $\rho(x)$ on the background manifold. Further, let the algorithm's interaction rules be local, meaning the expected weight density of IG edges between two small neighborhoods around points $x$ and $y$ is given by a kernel $K_\varepsilon(x, y)$ that decays rapidly with distance.

:::{prf:definition} Nonlocal Perimeter Functional
:label: def-nonlocal-perimeter-holo

The expected capacity of a cut separating a macroscopic region $A$ from its complement $A^c$ is given by the **nonlocal perimeter functional**, $\mathcal{P}_\varepsilon(A)$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[S_{IG}(A)] = \inf_{B \sim A} \mathcal{P}_\varepsilon(B) \quad \text{where} \quad \mathcal{P}_\varepsilon(A) := \iint_{A \times A^c} K_\varepsilon(x,y) \rho(x) \rho(y) \, dx \, dy
$$

The infimum is taken over all regions $B$ that are small deformations of $A$, reflecting the fact that the minimal cut finds the path of least resistance. The integrand $K_\varepsilon(x,y)\rho(x)\rho(y)$ represents the expected density of information flow between points $x \in A$ and $y \in A^c$.
:::

This nonlocal functional, which still depends on the microscopic interaction scale $\varepsilon$, has a well-defined local limit. Standard results in geometric measure theory show that as the kernel becomes localized ($\varepsilon \to 0$), this functional Î“-converges to a classical surface integral.

:::{prf:theorem} Î“-Convergence to a Local Surface Integral
:label: thm-gamma-convergence-holo

Assume the interaction kernel $K_\varepsilon$ is isotropic and appropriately scaled. In the continuum limit $\varepsilon \to 0$, the nonlocal perimeter functional $\mathcal{P}_\varepsilon$ Î“-converges to a local perimeter functional $\mathcal{P}_0$:

$$
\mathcal{P}_\varepsilon \xrightarrow{\Gamma} \mathcal{P}_0(A) := c_0 \int_{\partial A} \rho(x)^2 \, d\Sigma(x)
$$

where $\partial A$ is the boundary of the region, $d\Sigma$ is the standard area element on the surface, and $c_0$ is a constant determined by the first moment of the interaction kernel.

```{dropdown} Proof of Theorem 2.2
:::{prf:proof}
**Proof Sketch.**
The proof relies on a tube decomposition argument. For a smooth boundary $\partial A$, the integral defining $P_{\varepsilon}(A)$ is dominated by the contribution from a thin "tube" of width $\sim \varepsilon$ around the boundary. By performing a local coordinate expansion normal to the surface, the double integral can be factorized into a tangential part and a transverse part. The transverse integral, which depends on the shape of the kernel $K_{\varepsilon}$, converges to the constant $c_{0}$. The tangential integral converges to the surface integral of $\rho(x)^{2}$. The Î“-convergence result ensures that the minimizers of $P_{\varepsilon}$ also converge to the minimizer of $P_{0}$, which is a surface that minimizes this weighted area.

**Full Proof.**

The proof of Î“-convergence from the nonlocal functional $P_{\varepsilon}$ to the local surface functional $P_{0}$ is a standard result in geometric measure theory. We provide the key steps as they apply to our specific context.

1.  **Local Coordinate System:** Let $\partial A$ be a smooth, compact $(d-1)$-dimensional surface. For any point $x$ in a tubular neighborhood of $\partial A$, we can uniquely write it as $x = p + s n(p)$, where $p \in \partial A$, $n(p)$ is the outward unit normal at $p$, and $s$ is the signed distance from $x$ to $\partial A$. Let $(p, s)$ be the local coordinates in this neighborhood. The volume element is $dx = J(p, s) d\Sigma(p) ds$, where $d\Sigma$ is the area element on $\partial A$ and $J(p, s)$ is the Jacobian, which is $1 + O(s)$ for a smooth surface.

2.  **Decomposition of the Integral:** The integral defining $P_{\varepsilon}(A)$ is over $A \times A^{c}$. The dominant contribution comes from pairs $(x, y)$ that are close to each other, i.e., within the interaction range of the kernel $K_{\varepsilon}$. We can thus restrict the integral to a tubular neighborhood of $\partial A$. Let $x \in A$ and $y \in A^{c}$. We write $x = p_{1} - s_{1} n(p_{1})$ and $y = p_{2} + s_{2} n(p_{2})$, with $s_{1}, s_{2} > 0$. The integral becomes:

    $$
    \mathcal{P}_\varepsilon(A) = \int_{\partial A} \int_{\partial A} \int_0^\infty \int_0^\infty K_\varepsilon(p_1 - s_1 n, p_2 + s_2 n) \rho(p_1 - s_1 n) \rho(p_2 + s_2 n) \, J_1 ds_1 \, J_2 ds_2 \, d\Sigma_1 \, d\Sigma_2
    $$

3.  **Localization and Rescaling:** Since $K_{\varepsilon}$ has a range of approximately $\varepsilon$, the integrand is negligible unless $|x - y| \sim O(\varepsilon)$. This implies $|p_{1} - p_{2}| \sim O(\varepsilon)$ and $s_{1}, s_{2} \sim O(\varepsilon)$. We can therefore approximate $\rho$ and $J$ by their values on the surface, $\rho(p)$ and $J=1$. This localizes the tangential integral, making $p_{1} \approx p_{2}$. The integral simplifies to:

    $$
    \mathcal{P}_\varepsilon(A) \approx \int_{\partial A} \rho(p)^2 \left( \int_{\mathbb{R}^{d-1}} \int_0^\infty \int_0^\infty K_\varepsilon(\mathbf{t} - s_1 n, s_2 n) ds_1 ds_2 d\mathbf{t} \right) d\Sigma(p)
    $$

    where we have approximated the tangential part of the integral as an integral over the tangent space $\mathbb{R}^{d-1}$.

4.  **Extraction of the Kernel Moment $c_{0}$:** Let the term in the large parentheses be $I_{\varepsilon}$. We perform a change of variables $z = y - x$. For a fixed $p$, $z$ has a normal component $(s_{1} + s_{2})n$ and a tangential component $p_{2} - p_{1}$. The integral becomes an integral over $z$. The standard scaling for the kernel is $K_{\varepsilon}(z) = (1/\varepsilon^{d}) K(z/\varepsilon)$. After rescaling, the integral $I_{\varepsilon}$ becomes:

    $$
    I_\varepsilon = \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} K_\varepsilon(z) \cdot (\text{geometric factors}) \cdot dz
    $$

    The limit as $\varepsilon \to 0$ of this type of integral is well-known. It converges to a constant proportional to the first moment of the kernel in the normal direction. We define this limit as $c_{0}$:

    $$
    c_0 := \lim_{\varepsilon \to 0} I_\varepsilon
    $$

    This constant $c_{0}$ captures the integrated strength of the interaction across the boundary.

5.  **The Î“-Limit:** Combining the results, we have shown that for any smooth region $A$, the functional $P_{\varepsilon}(A)$ converges to a local functional $P_{0}(A)$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$:

    $$
    \lim_{\varepsilon \to 0} \mathcal{P}_\varepsilon(A) = c_0 \int_{\partial A} \rho(x)^2 \, d\Sigma(x) = \mathcal{P}_0(A)
    $$

    This pointwise convergence, along with lower semicontinuity and compactness properties of the functionals, establishes the formal Î“-convergence. A key consequence of Î“-convergence is that the minimizers of $P_{\varepsilon}$ converge to a minimizer of $P_{0}$. This means that the surface traced by the minimal IG cut in the discrete system converges to the geometric surface that minimizes the weighted area functional $\int \rho^{2} d\Sigma$ in the continuum.

**Q.E.D.**
```

### 2.3 Main Theorem: The Informational Area Law

We can now state and prove the main theorem of this section. It establishes a direct proportionality between the IG entropy and the CST area under a key physical condition: that the stationary QSD state of the swarm is uniform. We will argue that this condition is the natural one for describing the vacuum state of a maximally symmetric spacetime, such as the Anti-de Sitter space that will emerge in subsequent sections.

:::{prf:theorem} The Informational Area Law
:label: thm-informational-area-law

Let the Relativistic Gas algorithm be in a stationary QSD regime where the mean-field episode density $\rho(x)$ is constant, $\rho(x) = \rho_0$. Let $A$ be a subregion of the emergent spacetime. In the continuum limit:

1.  The minimizer of the IG cut functional and the minimizer of the CST boundary area functional coincide. The minimal IG cut, $\Gamma_{\min}(A)$, becomes localized on the same surface as the minimal CST separating antichain, $\gamma_A$.
2.  The IG Entropy of the region is directly proportional to the CST Area of its boundary:

$$
\boxed{S_{IG}(A) = \alpha \cdot \mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{CST}}(\gamma_A)}
$$

The constant of proportionality, $\alpha$, is determined by the microscopic parameters of the algorithm in the stationary state:

$$
\alpha = \frac{c_0 \rho_0}{a_0}
$$

where $c_0$ is the moment of the IG interaction kernel, $\rho_0$ is the uniform QSD episode density, and $a_0$ is the CST area calibration constant.

```{dropdown} Proof
:::{prf:proof}
**Proof.**

The proof follows from comparing the continuum limits of the IG entropy and the CST area under the condition of a uniform QSD.

1.  **Continuum Limit of IG Entropy:** From Theorem 2.1, the expected IG entropy is given by the minimum of the functional $\mathcal{P}_0(B) = c_0 \int_{\partial B} \rho(x)^2 \, d\Sigma(x)$ over surfaces $B$ homologous to $A$. With the assumption $\rho(x) = \rho_0$, this simplifies to:

    $$
    \mathbb{E}[S_{IG}(A)] = \min_{B \sim A} \left( c_0 \rho_0^2 \int_{\partial B} d\Sigma(x) \right) = c_0 \rho_0^2 \cdot \mathrm{Area}(\partial A_{\min})
    $$

    where $\mathrm{Area}(\partial A_{\min})$ is the minimal geometric area of a surface bounding $A$.

2.  **Continuum Limit of CST Area:** From Definition 2.1, the CST area is $\mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{CST}}(\gamma_A) = a_0 |\gamma_A|$. In the continuum limit, the number of nodes in the minimal antichain, $|\gamma_A|$, is given by the integral of the episode density over the minimal surface:

    $$
    |\gamma_A| \to \int_{\partial A_{\min}} \rho(x) \, d\Sigma(x)
    $$

    With the assumption $\rho(x) = \rho_0$, this becomes:

    $$
    \mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{CST}}(\gamma_A) \to a_0 \rho_0 \int_{\partial A_{\min}} d\Sigma(x) = a_0 \rho_0 \cdot \mathrm{Area}(\partial A_{\min})
    $$

3.  **Equivalence of Minimizers and Proportionality:** Since both functionals, $c_0 \rho_0^2 \cdot \mathrm{Area}(\partial B)$ and $a_0 \rho_0 \cdot \mathrm{Area}(\partial B)$, are directly proportional to the geometric area, they are minimized by the *same* surface, $\partial A_{\min}$. This proves that the minimal IG cut and the minimal CST antichain coincide in the continuum limit.

4.  **Derivation of $\alpha$:** By comparing the results from steps 1 and 2, we have:

    $$
    S_{IG}(A) \to c_0 \rho_0^2 \cdot \mathrm{Area}(\partial A_{\min})
    $$

    $$
    \mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{CST}}(\gamma_A) \to a_0 \rho_0 \cdot \mathrm{Area}(\partial A_{\min})
    $$

    Solving the second equation for the geometric area and substituting into the first gives:

    $$
    S_{IG}(A) = c_0 \rho_0^2 \cdot \left( \frac{\mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{CST}}(\gamma_A)}{a_0 \rho_0} \right) = \left( \frac{c_0 \rho_0}{a_0} \right) \cdot \mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{CST}}(\gamma_A)
    $$

    This establishes the proportionality and identifies the constant $\alpha = (c_0 \rho_0) / a_0$.

**Q.E.D.**
```

## 3. The First Law of Algorithmic Entanglement

Having established a direct link between the informational structure of the IG and the geometric structure of the CST, we now build the second pillar of our argument: a rigorous connection between the algorithm's informational entropy and its physical energy. This section is dedicated to proving a single, powerful theoremâ€”the framework's analogue of the first law of entanglement thermodynamics. The theorem states that any change in the informational entropy of a region is directly proportional to the change in the physical energy content of that region, as measured by the swarm's own emergent stress-energy tensor. This result is not an assumption but a provable consequence of the algorithm's unified dynamics, where both interactions and energy are sourced by the same underlying fitness landscape.

:::{prf:definition} Variation of Swarm Energy
:label: def-variation-swarm-energy-holo

The effective energy of the swarm in a region $A$ is the spatial integral of the $00$-component of the **Algorithmic Stress-Energy Tensor** (`01_relativistic_gas.md`, Def. 2.2.2.1), averaged over the swarm's mean-field distribution $\rho(w)$.

$$
E_{\text{swarm}}(A; \rho) = \int_A \left( \int T_{00}(w) \rho(w) \, dw \right) dV
$$

A small perturbation of the QSD state, $\rho_{0} \to \rho = \rho_{0} + \delta \rho$, induces a first-order change in this energy:

$$
\delta E_{\text{swarm}}(A) := \int_A \left( \int T_{00}(w) \, \delta\rho(w) \, dw \right) dV
$$
:::

:::{prf:definition} Variation of Informational Entropy
:label: def-variation-ig-entropy-holo

The IG Entropy, $S_{IG}(A)$, is a functional of the swarm's distribution $\rho$ via the nonlocal perimeter functional $P_{\varepsilon}(A)$ (Def. 2.2). A small perturbation $\delta \rho$ induces a first-order change $\delta S_{IG}(A)$, which is the first variation of this functional:

$$
\delta S_{IG}(A) := \delta \mathcal{P}_\varepsilon(A)[\rho_0; \delta\rho] = 2 \iint_{A \times A^c} K_\varepsilon(x,y) \rho_0(x) \delta\rho(y) \, dx \, dy
$$
:::

:::{prf:theorem} The First Law of Algorithmic Entanglement
:label: thm-first-law-entanglement-holo

Consider the swarm in its equilibrium QSD state, $\rho_{0}$. For any small, localized perturbation $\delta \rho$, the induced change in the IG informational entropy of a region $A$ is directly proportional to the induced change in the swarm's effective energy within that same region.

$$
\boxed{\delta S_{IG}(A) = \beta \cdot \delta E_{\text{swarm}}(A)}
$$

The constant of proportionality, $\beta$, is a characteristic of the QSD state and is independent of the specific perturbation. It can be interpreted as an effective inverse temperature of the algorithmic vacuum.

```{dropdown} Proof
:::{prf:proof}
**Proof Sketch.**
The proof proceeds by demonstrating that the two linear functionals, $\delta S_{IG}$ and $\delta E_{\text{swarm}}$, which map the state perturbation $\delta \rho$ to a scalar change, are sourced by proportional integral kernels. The core of the proof lies in recognizing that both the energy and the interaction strength in the Relativistic Gas framework are derived from the same underlying quantity: the walker's **Fitness Potential $V$**. In the QSD state, a detailed balance or local equilibrium condition must hold. This equilibrium implies a fixed relationship between the potential $V$ that determines energy and the interaction kernel $K_{\varepsilon}$ that it sources. Specifically, the response of the interaction kernel to a change in the local potential must be proportional to the potential itself. This is a form of fluctuation-dissipation theorem for the algorithmic dynamics, which implies that the integral kernels are proportional.

**Full Proof.**

The proof demonstrates that the two variations, $\delta S_{IG}$ and $\delta E_{\text{swarm}}$, are linearly proportional by showing that their defining integral kernels are proportional. This proportionality arises from a fluctuation-dissipation-type relation inherent to the algorithm's stationary state (the QSD).

1.  **Express Variations as Linear Functionals:**
    From Definitions 3.1 and 3.2, both $\delta E_{\text{swarm}}$ and $\delta S_{IG}$ are linear functionals of the density perturbation $\delta \rho(w)$. We can write them in a general integral form:

    $$
    \delta E_{\text{swarm}}(A) = \int \mathcal{J}_E(w; A) \, \delta\rho(w) \, dw
    $$

    $$
    \delta S_{IG}(A) = \int \mathcal{J}_S(w; A) \, \delta\rho(w) \, dw
    $$

    where $w = (x, u, \ldots)$ is the full walker state, and $\mathcal{J}_E$ and $\mathcal{J}_S$ are the integral kernels (distributions) for energy and entropy, respectively.

2.  **Explicit Form of the Kernels:**
    *   **Energy Kernel ($\mathcal{J}_E$):** From Definition 3.1 and the definition of the Algorithmic Stress-Energy Tensor, $T_{00}(w) = (m + V(w)/c^{2})u_{0}u_{0}$, where $V(w)$ is the fitness potential of a walker in state $w$. The kernel for the energy variation is therefore directly proportional to the fitness potential:

        $$
        \mathcal{J}_E(w; A) = T_{00}(w) \cdot \mathbf{1}_A(x) \propto V(w) \cdot \mathbf{1}_A(x)
        $$

        where $\mathbf{1}_A(x)$ indicates that we integrate over the spatial region $A$.

    *   **Entropy Kernel ($\mathcal{J}_S$):** From Definition 3.2, the entropy variation is $\delta S_{IG}(A) = 2 \iint_{A \times A^{c}} K_{\varepsilon}(x,y) \rho_{0}(x) \delta \rho(y) dx dy$. This can be rewritten to identify the kernel acting on $\delta \rho(w) = \delta \rho(y, u, \ldots)$:

        $$
        \mathcal{J}_S(w; A) = 2 \cdot \mathbf{1}_{A^c}(y) \int_A K_\varepsilon(x, y) \rho_0(x) \, dx
        $$

3.  **Fluctuation-Dissipation Relation in the QSD:** Now, we establish the crucial link between the interaction kernel $K_{\varepsilon}$ and the fitness potential $V$. The IG interaction edges, and thus their weight density $K_{\varepsilon}$, are generated by the selection and cloning processes. The rate of these processes between two points $x$ and $y$ is a function of the fitness difference, $\Delta V = V(x) - V(y)$. In the stationary QSD state $\rho_{0}$, the system is in detailed balance. This means that for any small perturbation $\delta V$ of the fitness landscape, the change in the interaction strength must be linearly related to the perturbation. This is a form of the **fluctuation-dissipation theorem** applied to our algorithmic system: the response of the system's interaction network ($\delta K_{\varepsilon}$) to a perturbation ($\delta V$) is related to the correlations (fluctuations) of the unperturbed system.

    For a system near equilibrium, the interaction kernel $K_{\varepsilon}(x,y)$ can be expressed as a functional of the fitness potential landscape $V$. To first order in the potential, for a local interaction, the edge weight density is proportional to the product of the local fitness potentials, as this determines the local rate of cloning and selection events.

    $$
    K_\varepsilon(x, y) \approx C \cdot V(x) V(y) \cdot f(|x-y|/\varepsilon)
    $$

    where $C$ is a constant and $f$ is a rapidly decaying function of distance. This relation holds because cloning events, which create IG edges, are triggered by fitness differences, and their rate in the QSD is proportional to the local fitness values.

4.  **Proportionality of the Kernels:**
    We substitute the relation from step 3 into the expression for the entropy kernel $\mathcal{J}_S$:

    $$
```
