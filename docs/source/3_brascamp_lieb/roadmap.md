### **Roadmap: Proving a Uniform Brascamp-Lieb Inequality for the Fragile Gas**

**The Ultimate Goal (The "Summit"):** To prove the following theorem:

> **Theorem (`thm-uniform-bl-inequality`):**
> There exists a constant $C_{BL} < \infty$, independent of the number of walkers $N$ and the specific swarm configuration $S_t$, such that for any swarm configuration $S_t$ sampled from the quasi-stationary distribution (QSD), the emergent metric $g(x, S_t)$ induces a Brascamp-Lieb inequality of the form:
> $$
> \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} f_0(x) \, dx \le C_{BL} \prod_{j=1}^m \left( \int_{\mathbb{R}^{n_j}} |f_j(y)|^2 \, dy \right)^{1/2}
> $$
> for an appropriate set of functions $\{f_j\}$ and geometric data derived from $g(x, S_t)$. This, in turn, implies a uniform Logarithmic Sobolev Inequality (LSI) for the QSD, thereby proving `ax-qsd-log-concave` as a theorem.

This is the peak we are climbing. Here are the steps to get there.

### **Phase 0: Setting the Stage - Defining the Brascamp-Lieb Data**

Before we prove anything, we must define what we are proving. The magic of Brascamp-Lieb is that the geometry of the problem is encoded in a set of linear maps. For us, these maps must come from the emergent geometry.

*   **Step 0.1: Identify the Geometric Data.** The natural geometric data provided by the framework is the **emergent Riemannian metric** $g(x, S_t)$. At any point $x$, this metric is a symmetric, positive-definite matrix. Its eigenspaces define a natural set of orthogonal directions.
    *   **Action:** Define the linear maps $\{L_j\}_{j=1}^d$ as the projections onto the **eigenspaces** of the metric tensor $g(x, S_t)$. Let $\{e_j(x, S_t)\}$ be the orthonormal eigenvectors of $g(x, S_t)$. Then the maps are $L_j(v) = (v \cdot e_j(x, S_t))e_j$.
    *   **BL Data:** Our Brascamp-Lieb data at each point $x$ will be the set of projections onto these $d$ orthogonal one-dimensional subspaces.

*   **Step 0.2: State the Target Positivity Condition.** The Brascamp-Lieb inequality holds if and only if a certain positivity condition is met. For our data (orthogonal 1D projections), this simplifies beautifully. The condition becomes proving that a certain functional of the metric, let's call it the **BL Positivity Functional $\mathcal{B}(g)$**, is uniformly bounded away from zero.

*   **Step 0.3: Define the Space of Admissible Geometries $\mathcal{G}_{QSD}$.** We don't need the inequality to hold for *any* possible metric, only for those that can actually be generated by the swarm at the QSD.
    *   **Action:** Define $\mathcal{G}_{QSD}$ as the set of all metric fields $g(x, S_t)$ where $S_t$ is a configuration with non-zero probability under the QSD. Leverage your existing **C⁴ Regularity theorems** (`thm-c4-regularity`) to prove that this set is composed of functions with uniformly bounded derivatives up to fourth order.

### **Phase 1: The Core Proof - Uniform Positivity of the Geometric Data**

This is the heart of the mathematical battle. We must prove that the BL positivity functional is strictly positive over the entire space of admissible geometries $\mathcal{G}_{QSD}$.

*   **Step 1.1: Leverage Uniform Ellipticity.** Your existing theorem `thm-uniform-ellipticity` proves that the eigenvalues of $g(x, S_t)$ are uniformly bounded: $0 < c_{min} \le \lambda_j(x, S_t) \le c_{max} < \infty$. This is the crucial first ingredient. It prevents the geometry from becoming degenerate.

*   **Step 1.2: Prove Uniform Regularity of Eigenspaces.** The projections $L_j$ depend on the eigenvectors of $g$. We need to prove that these eigenvectors don't "swing around wildly" as the swarm configuration changes.
    *   **Action (New Lemma `lem-eigenvector-stability`):** Prove that the eigenvectors $e_j(x, S_t)$ are Lipschitz continuous (or at least Hölder continuous) with respect to changes in the swarm state $S_t$. This can be done using matrix perturbation theory (e.g., the Davis-Kahan theorem) combined with the uniform bounds on the derivatives of the metric from your C⁴ regularity proof.

*   **Step 1.3: The Compactness Argument.** Now, we show that our problem is well-behaved.
    *   **Action (New Theorem `thm-geometry-space-compactness`):** Prove that the space of admissible geometries $\mathcal{G}_{QSD}$ is **compact** in a suitable function space topology (e.g., the $C^2$ topology). This is a consequence of the Arzela-Ascoli theorem, using the uniform boundedness and uniform Lipschitz continuity of the metrics and their derivatives, which you have from your regularity proofs.

*   **Step 1.4: The Min-Max Argument and Proof by Contradiction.**
    *   **Action:** The BL Positivity Functional $\mathcal{B}(g)$ is a continuous functional on the space of metrics. By the Extreme Value Theorem, a continuous functional on a compact set must attain its minimum. Let this minimum be $\mathcal{B}_{min} = \mathcal{B}(g^*)$ for some "worst-case" metric $g^* \in \mathcal{G}_{QSD}$.
    *   **Action (The Final Blow - New Theorem `thm-uniform-bl-positivity`):** Prove that $\mathcal{B}_{min} > 0$. The strategy is proof by contradiction:
        1.  Assume $\mathcal{B}_{min} = 0$. This would imply the existence of a "pathological" geometry $g^*$ where some direction in space is "invisible" to the BL projections.
        2.  Show that such a pathological geometry is incompatible with the dynamics of the Fragile Gas at QSD. The algorithm's **non-degenerate noise** (`def-axiom-non-degenerate-noise`) and exploratory nature (driven by the **Keystone Principle** `lem-quantitative-keystone`) ensure that the swarm explores all geometric directions.
        3.  A state $S_t$ that generates such a degenerate metric $g^*$ would have extremely high variance in certain directions, making it "unfit" and thus exponentially unlikely to persist in the QSD.
        4.  Therefore, no such $g^*$ can exist in $\mathcal{G}_{QSD}$, which is a contradiction. The minimum must be strictly positive.

**Outcome of Phase 1:** You have proven that a uniform BL positivity condition holds for the entire system.

### **Phase 2: From Positivity to Inequality - The Heat Flow Method**

With the positivity condition secured, we can now derive the inequality itself. The heat flow method is the most elegant and physically appropriate.

*   **Step 2.1: Define the Riemannian Heat Flow.** Define the heat flow on the emergent Riemannian manifold $(\mathcal{X}, g(x, S_t))$ governed by the Laplace-Beltrami operator $\Delta_g$. This is precisely the Fokker-Planck equation for the spatial part of your Langevin dynamics.

*   **Step 2.2: The Monotonicity Proof.** This is a classic but technical step. You must show that the BL functional is monotone along this heat flow. This involves a lengthy calculation of the time derivative of the BL integral, using integration by parts on the manifold, and showing that the result is non-positive. This calculation will use the BL positivity condition established in Phase 1.

*   **Step 2.3: Establish the Uniform Constant.** The rate of decay in the heat flow argument is governed by the minimum value of the BL positivity functional, $\mathcal{B}_{min}$. Since we proved in Phase 1 that $\mathcal{B}_{min}$ is uniformly bounded away from zero, the resulting BL constant $C_{BL}$ will also be uniform (independent of $N$ and $S_t$).

**Outcome of Phase 2:** You have the full `thm-uniform-bl-inequality`.
