\title{Lattice Gauge Theory on the Interaction Complex\\
of a Viscous Particle System}
\author{}
\date{}
\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We construct a finite, explicit $SU(3)$ lattice gauge theory from the discrete
trajectory data of a viscous multi-particle system.
Starting from a recorded run of the interacticle algorithm, we extract a directed
2-complex whose nodes are spacetime particle states, whose edges encode causal
propagation and pairwise viscous interactions, and whose triangular faces close
the interaction-ahead boundaries.
At each node we form a complex color triplet by modulating the viscous force
vector with a kinematic phase factor of de~Broglie type; these triplets encode
momentum direction in the internal color space.
Edge-to-edge parallel transport in $SU(3)$ is then defined by transporting
color frames induced by the color triplets across oriented edges.
Wilson loop traces, the standard plaquette action, and covariant fermionic and
scalar matter actions are constructed on the same complex.
The complete data package $\mathcal Q$ is proved to be finite and $SU(3)$
gauge-invariant.
Continuum limit, path-integral quantization, and axiom verification are
deferred to companion papers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Lattice gauge theory, introduced by Wilson~\cite{wilson1974confinement}, provides
a non-perturbative regularization of quantum field theories by replacing
continuous spacetime with a discrete lattice and assigning $SU(N)$ group elements
to the links.
In the standard formulation the link variables are
\emph{independent integration variables} of a path integral, and the gauge group
is imposed by hand.
The present paper takes a different starting point: we ask whether a
natural $SU(3)$ gauge structure can be \emph{extracted} from the discrete
trajectory data of an interacting classical system, without imposing any gauge
group a priori.

The data source is the interacticle algorithm, a multi-particle sampler in which
walkers move through a latent space under underdamped Langevin dynamics with a
row-normalized viscous alignment force~\cite{creutz1983quarks}.
At each discrete timestep, each alive walker $i$ experiences a force
\[
  \mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}_i
  = \nu_{\mathrm{visc}}\sum_{j\neq i}\omega_{ij}(v_j - v_i)
\]
that pulls its velocity toward a weighted average of its interaction partners.
This force is a vector in $\R^3$ and therefore lives naturally in the fundamental
representation of $O(3)$.

The key observation is that $\R^3$ is three-dimensional, matching the defining
dimension of the fundamental representation of $SU(3)$, the gauge group of the
strong nuclear force~\cite{gellmann1962symmetries}.
By complexifying the viscous force with a kinematic phase factor analogous to the
de~Broglie phase $e^{ip\ell/\hbar}$, each spacetime node acquires a complex
color triplet~$\mathbf c_x\in\C^3$.
The color triplets at two neighboring nodes define interaction data from which we
build an \(SU(3)\) parallel transport \(U_3(e)\) for every edge \(e\) by a local
frame construction.
No additional input is required: the gauge group, the link variables, and
the coupling strength all emerge from the dynamics of the viscous particle system.

In detail, we first build a deterministic \(SU(3)\) frame at each node from the
color triplet, then define \(U_3\) as the frame transition between source and
target nodes.
The same data can be decomposed in the Gell-Mann basis after taking the matrix
logarithm. The coupling parameter \(g_3>0\) is placed to match the continuum
normalization in
$D_\mu=\partial_\mu-ig_3 A_\mu^a\lambda_a/2$.

This paper supplies all the formal definitions and proofs required for a
subsequent axiom-verification paper.
The main result is Theorem~\ref{thm:package}: the tuple
$\mathcal Q = (\text{complex},\,\{U_3(e)\},\,S_\mathrm W,\,S_\mathrm F,\,S_\phi)$
is finite, explicit, and $SU(3)$ gauge-invariant.

\section{Notation and setup}
\label{sec:notation}

\begin{definition}[Kernel parameters and trajectory data]
\label{def:imports}
Let $d=3$.
The spatial dimension is fixed to three throughout because the viscous force is
a vector in physical space and its three components form a natural triplet
for the fundamental representation of $SU(3)$.
Fix $N\ge1$ and the index set $\mathcal I:=\{1,\dots,N\}$.
At each time every trajectory entry is recorded for all $i\in\mathcal I$.
The alive set $\mathcal A(t)\subseteq\mathcal I$ records which indices are active.
Fix kernel parameters
\[
\ell_{\mathrm{visc}}>0,\qquad \kappa_0>0,\qquad \nu_{\mathrm{visc}}\ge0,
\]
and define the regularized Gaussian kernel
\[
K_\rho(x,y):=\kappa_0+\exp\!\left(-\frac{\|x-y\|^2}{2\ell_{\mathrm{visc}}^2}\right),
\qquad \rho:=\ell_{\mathrm{visc}}.
\]
The kernel satisfies
\[
\kappa_*:=\inf_{x,y}K_\rho(x,y)=\kappa_0>0,\qquad
\kappa^*:=\sup_{x,y}K_\rho(x,y)=1+\kappa_0<\infty.
\]
These bounds are imported from the companion paper on kinetic convergence;
no other results from that paper are used here.

A recorded trajectory is available at discrete times
\[
\Tset=\{0,1,\dots,T\},\quad T\in\N,
\]
with finite alive sets $\mathcal A(t)\subseteq\mathcal I$ and position,
velocity, and fitness data
\[
x_i(t),\,v_i(t)\in\R^3,\qquad \Phi_i(t)\in\R,\qquad i\in\mathcal I,\ t\in\Tset.
\]
At each timestep $t$, the finite set
$\mathcal P_t\subseteq \mathcal A(t)\times\mathcal A(t)\setminus\{(i,i):i\in\mathcal A(t)\}$
records the ordered interaction companions used by the algorithm.
\end{definition}

together with their role and the constraint they must satisfy.

\begin{lemma}[Kernel orthogonal symmetry]
\label{lem:kernel-orth}
For every orthogonal matrix $O\in O(3)$ and all $x,y\in\R^3$,
\[
K_\rho(Ox,Oy)=K_\rho(x,y).
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
\|Ox-Oy\|^2=\|O(x-y)\|^2=\|x-y\|^2,
\]
so the Gaussian factor is invariant under simultaneous orthogonal transformation.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Orthogonal covariance of viscous forces]
\label{lem:orthogonal}
Let $O\in O(3)$ and define transformed data $x_i'(t)=Ox_i(t)$, $v_i'(t)=Ov_i(t)$.
Then for each node $x=n_{i,t}$,
\[
\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x;\text{transformed data})=O\,\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x;\text{original data}).
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Lemma~\ref{lem:kernel-orth} gives $K_\rho(Ox_i,Ox_j)=K_\rho(x_i,x_j)$, hence
\[
\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}'(i,t)=\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t),
\qquad
\omega_{ij}^{\mathrm{IG}\prime}(t)=\omega_{ij}^{\mathrm{IG}}(t).
\]
For each edge $e=(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t})$,
\[
\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}{}'(e)
=\nu_{\mathrm{visc}}\,\omega^{\mathrm{IG}}_{ij}(t)(Ov_j-Ov_i)
=O\,\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(e).
\]
Summing outgoing IG edges at a fixed source node preserves left multiplication by $O$.
\end{proof}

\section{Recorded run and interaction complex}
\label{sec:run}
\begin{definition}[Recorded interacticle run]
\label{def:run}
The recorded run is the tuple
\[
\mathscr R=\Bigl(
N,\Tset,\{\mathcal A(t)\}_{t\in\Tset},\{\mathcal P_t\}_{t\in\Tset},
\{x_i(t),v_i(t),\Phi_i(t)\}_{i\in\mathcal I,t\in\Tset},\{K_\rho\}\Bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Node set and projections]
\label{def:nodes}
For each pair $(i,t)\in\mathcal I\times\Tset$ define a spacetime node $n_{i,t}$ and set
\[
\mcN:=\{n_{i,t}\mid i\in\mathcal I,\ t\in\Tset\}.
\]
Define spatial and velocity projections
\[
\mathbf x(n_{i,t})=x_i(t),\qquad \mathbf v(n_{i,t})=v_i(t).
\]
Both maps are well-defined by definition of $\mcN$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Oriented edge sets]
\label{def:edges}
Define three families of oriented edges:
\begin{align*}
E_{\mathrm{CST}}&:=\{(n_{i,t}\to n_{i,t+1}):\,i\in\mathcal A(t),\ t=0,\dots,T-1\},\\
E_{\mathrm{IG}}&:=\{(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}):\,(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t,\ t\in\Tset\},\\
E_{\mathrm{IA}}&:=\{(n_{i,t+1}\to n_{j,t}):\,(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t,\ t=0,\dots,T-1\}.
\end{align*}
For convenience, write
\[
e_{\mathrm{CST}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{i,t+1}) := (n_{i,t}\to n_{i,t+1}),\quad
e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}) := (n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}),\quad
e_{\mathrm{IA}}(n_{i,t+1}\to n_{j,t}) := (n_{i,t+1}\to n_{j,t}).
\]
By construction all source and target nodes of $E_{\mathrm{IA}}$ lie in $\mcN$
because $\mcN$ is defined for all $(i,t)\in\mathcal I\times\Tset$.
The causal set tree (CST) edges $E_{\mathrm{CST}}$ encode temporal propagation:
walker $i$ at time $t$ evolves forward to time $t+1$.
The interaction graph (IG) edges $E_{\mathrm{IG}}$ encode same-time viscous
couplings: walker $i$ interacts with companion $j$ at the same timestep.
The interaction-ahead (IA) edges $E_{\mathrm{IA}}$ encode forward-in-time
interactions: the updated state of $i$ at $t+1$ is causally linked back to the
companion $j$ at~$t$.
Set
\[
\mcE:=E_{\mathrm{CST}}\cup E_{\mathrm{IG}}\cup E_{\mathrm{IA}}.
\]
For $e=(u\to v)\in\mcE$, write $s(e):=u$ and $t(e):=v$, and define $e^{-1}:=(t(e)\to s(e))$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Interaction triangles and plaquettes]
\label{def:triangles}
For each $(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t$ with $t<T$, define the oriented interaction triangle
\[
\triangle_{ij,t}:=(n_{i,t},n_{i,t+1},n_{j,t}),
\]
with oriented boundary
\[
\partial\triangle_{ij,t}=e_{\mathrm{CST}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{i,t+1})
+e_{\mathrm{IA}}(n_{i,t+1}\to n_{j,t})-e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}).
\]
If both ordered pairs $(i,j),(j,i)\in\mathcal P_t$ with $t<T$, define the plaquette
\[
P_{ij,t}:=\triangle_{ij,t}\cup \triangle_{ji,t}
\]
with boundary 4-cycle
\[
\partial P_{ij,t}=e_{\mathrm{CST}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{i,t+1})
+e_{\mathrm{IA}}(n_{i,t+1}\to n_{j,t})
+e_{\mathrm{CST}}(n_{j,t}\to n_{j,t+1})
+e_{\mathrm{IA}}(n_{j,t+1}\to n_{i,t}).
\]
The IG contributions from the two triangles cancel in the plaquette boundary
because they appear with opposite signs.
Set
\[
\mcT:=\{\triangle_{ij,t}\mid (i,j)\in\mathcal P_t,\ t=0,\dots,T-1\},\qquad
\mathcal F:=(\mcN,\mcE,\mcT).
\]
Define the underlying undirected structure
\[
\bar E_{\mathrm{CST}}:=\{\{u,v\}:(u\to v)\in E_{\mathrm{CST}}\},\quad
\bar E_{\mathrm{IG}}:=\{\{u,v\}:(u\to v)\in E_{\mathrm{IG}}\},\quad
\bar E_{\mathrm{IA}}:=\{\{u,v\}:(u\to v)\in E_{\mathrm{IA}}\},
\]
\[
\bar E:=\bar E_{\mathrm{CST}}\cup\bar E_{\mathrm{IG}}\cup\bar E_{\mathrm{IA}},\qquad
\mathcal F_{\mathrm{und}}:=(\mcN,\bar E,\mcT).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Directed 2-complex structure]
\label{prop:complex}
$\mathcal F_{\mathrm{und}}$ is a finite undirected 2-complex.
For each oriented edge $e=(u\to v)\in\mcE$ define $\partial_1 e=v-u$
(in the free $\Z$-module on $\mcN$), and for each triangle define
\[
\partial_2\triangle_{ij,t}
=e_{\mathrm{CST}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{i,t+1})
+e_{\mathrm{IA}}(n_{i,t+1}\to n_{j,t})
-e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}).
\]
For each $t<T$ with $(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t$,
\[
\partial_1\partial_2\triangle_{ij,t}=0,
\]
and the plaquette boundary satisfies $\partial P_{ij,t}=\partial\triangle_{ij,t}+\partial\triangle_{ji,t}$,
with the shared IG edges canceling by opposite orientations.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each node, edge, and face is built from the finite sets of
Definitions~\ref{def:run}--\ref{def:triangles}, so finiteness is clear.
The boundary-of-boundary identity $\partial_1\partial_2=0$ follows by direct
computation:
\[
\partial_1\partial_2\triangle_{ij,t}
=(n_{i,t+1}-n_{i,t})+(n_{j,t}-n_{i,t+1})-(n_{j,t}-n_{i,t})=0.
\]
In the sum defining $\partial P_{ij,t}$, the IG edge $e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t})$
from $\partial_2\triangle_{ij,t}$ appears with coefficient $-1$, while
$e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{j,t}\to n_{i,t})$ from $\partial_2\triangle_{ji,t}$ is the
reverse edge, hence it appears with coefficient $+1$ but represents the same
undirected edge.
In the oriented sum these contributions cancel, leaving only CST and IA edges.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Finiteness and cardinality]
\label{prop:finiteness}
All sets in Definitions~\ref{def:run}--\ref{def:triangles} are finite.
With $m_t:=|\mathcal P_t|$ and
\[
E_{\mathrm{IG}}^{<T}:=\{(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}):\,(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t,\ t\in\{0,\dots,T-1\}\},
\]
the cardinalities satisfy
\[
|E_{\mathrm{CST}}|=\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}|\mathcal A(t)|,\qquad
|E_{\mathrm{IG}}^{<T}|=|\mcT|=|E_{\mathrm{IA}}|=\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}m_t,\qquad
|E_{\mathrm{IG}}|=\sum_{t=0}^{T}m_t.
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Finiteness follows from finiteness of $\Tset$, all $\mathcal A(t)$, and all $\mathcal P_t$.
The cardinality identities hold by one-to-one correspondence: each
$(i,t)\in\bigcup_{t=0}^{T-1}\mathcal A(t)\times\{t\}$ contributes one CST edge,
and each $(i,j,t)\in\bigcup_t\mathcal P_t\times\{t<T\}$ contributes one IG (pre-$T$),
IA, and triangle.
The last timestep $t=T$ contributes $m_T$ additional IG edges.
\end{proof}

\section{Viscous force from interaction edges}
\label{sec:forces}

\begin{definition}[IG degree and pairwise force coefficients]
\label{def:ig-weights}
For each $(i,t)$ define the raw IG degree and normalized weights
\[
\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t):=\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t}K_\rho(x_i(t),x_j(t)),
\]
\[
\omega^{\mathrm{IG}}_{ij}(t):=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{K_\rho(x_i(t),x_j(t))}{\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t)},&(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t,\ \deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t)>0,\\[0.3em]
0,&\text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Viscous pair force and node force]
\label{def:forces}
For each edge $e=(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t})\in E_{\mathrm{IG}}$ define
\[
\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(e):=\nu_{\mathrm{visc}}\omega^{\mathrm{IG}}_{ij}(t)\,(v_j(t)-v_i(t))\in\R^3.
\]
For $x=n_{i,t}\in\mcN$ define
\[
\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x):=\sum_{\substack{e\in E_{\mathrm{IG}}\\ s(e)=x}}\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(e).
\]
If the sum is over an empty set, it is $0$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Weight properties]
\label{lem:weights}
For every $i\in\mathcal A(t)$ and $t\in\Tset$,
\[
\omega^{\mathrm{IG}}_{ij}(t)\ge0,\qquad
\sum_{j:(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t}\omega^{\mathrm{IG}}_{ij}(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t)>0\}}.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Positivity is immediate from $K_\rho\ge\kappa_*>0$.
If $\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t)>0$, summing the definitions over $j$ gives
\[
\sum_{j:(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t}\omega^{\mathrm{IG}}_{ij}(t)
=\frac{1}{\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t)}\sum_{j:(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t}K_\rho(x_i(t),x_j(t))=1.
\]
If $\deg_{\mathrm{IG}}(i,t)=0$, every summand is $0$ by convention.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Node reconstruction of viscous force]
\label{lem:reconstruction}
For every $x=n_{i,t}\in\mcN$,
\[
\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x)=\sum_{\substack{e\in E_{\mathrm{IG}}\\ s(e)=x}}\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(e).
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By Definition~\ref{def:edges}, the edges with source $n_{i,t}$ in $E_{\mathrm{IG}}$
are exactly $\{(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}):(i,j)\in\mathcal P_t\}$, which is the same
index set used in Definition~\ref{def:forces}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Orthogonal basis covariance]
\label{thm:viscous-su3}
Let $O\in O(3)$ and consider transformed data
$x_i'(t)=Ox_i(t)$, $v_i'(t)=Ov_i(t)$.
For every node $x\in\mcN$,
\[
\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x;\text{transformed data})=O\,\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x;\text{original data}).
\]
Moreover, real orthogonal matrices satisfy $O^\top=O^{-1}$, hence $O^\dagger=O^{-1}$,
so $O\in U(3)$.
Consequently, orthogonal changes of the physical coordinate frame embed in
unitary changes of the complexified color index.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Covariance of the force is Lemma~\ref{lem:orthogonal}.
For the embedding: since $O$ has real entries, $O^\dagger=O^\top=O^{-1}$,
so $O^\dagger O=I$ and $O$ is unitary when viewed as a complex matrix.
\end{proof}

\section{Color triplets and $SU(3)$ edge transport}
\label{sec:su3}

\begin{definition}[Normalized color vectors]
\label{def:colors}
Fix constants $g_3>0,\ \ell_0>0,\ \hbar_{\mathrm{eff}}>0,\ \mu_p>0,\ \epsilon_c>0$.
For $x=n_{i,t}\in\mcN$ and each coordinate $\alpha\in\{1,2,3\}$, define
\[
\tilde c_x^{(\alpha)}:=(\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x))_\alpha
\exp\!\left(i\frac{\mu_p\,v_i^{(\alpha)}(t)\ell_0}{\hbar_{\mathrm{eff}}}\right),
\]
\[
\mathbf c_x:=\frac{\tilde{\mathbf c}_x}{\sqrt{\tilde{\mathbf c}_x^\dagger\tilde{\mathbf c}_x+\epsilon_c^2}}\in\C^3.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Emergent local $SU(3)$ color symmetry]
\label{thm:viscous-su3-local}
For any map $\Omega:\mcN\to O(3)$ and each node $x\in\mcN$,
\[
\mathbf c_x^\Omega:=\Omega(x)\mathbf c_x.
\]
Because $\Omega(x)$ is real orthogonal, $\|\mathbf c_x^\Omega\|=\|\mathbf c_x\|$ for every
$x\in\mcN$.
Hence the color data carry a local $O(3)$ redundancy, which embeds as real
unitaries into $U(3)$.
Imposing $\det\Omega(x)=1$ for every node yields a local $SU(3)$ redundancy
on the color triplet bundle used below.
\begin{proof}
\[
\mathbf c_x^\Omega
=\frac{\Omega(x)\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x)\odot
\exp\!\left(i\mu_p\,\mathbf v(x)\,\ell_0/\hbar_{\mathrm{eff}}\right)}
{\sqrt{\|\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x)\|^2+\epsilon_c^2}}
\]
where $\odot$ denotes elementwise multiplication.
By definition this equals $\Omega(x)\mathbf c_x$.
Since $\Omega(x)$ is orthogonal, $\|\Omega(x)\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x)\|
=\|\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x)\|$.
Therefore the normalization denominator is unchanged and
$\|\mathbf c_x^\Omega\|=\|\mathbf c_x\|$.
\end{proof}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Physical interpretation of the color construction]
\label{rem:color-physics}
The factor
$\exp\!\bigl(i\mu_p v_i^{(\alpha)} \ell_0/\hbar_{\mathrm{eff}}\bigr)$
is a discrete analogue of the de~Broglie phase $e^{ip\ell/\hbar}$:
it encodes the kinematic momentum of walker $i$ in the $\alpha$-direction into the
phase of the $\alpha$-th color component.
Nodes moving in different directions therefore carry genuinely different
internal phases, even if they experience the same force magnitude.
This momentum-phase coupling ensures that the resulting parallel transport
$U_3(e)$ depends not only on the force correlation between nodes but also
on their relative kinematic state.

The regularization $\epsilon_c>0$ ensures that $\mathbf c_x$ is well-defined
even when the viscous force vanishes, e.g.\ for an isolated walker or when
$\nu_{\mathrm{visc}}=0$.
If $\mathbf c_x=0$, then $\Re(\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y)=0$
for all outgoing edges from $x$, hence $U_3(x\to y)=I$ for all such edges.
The same holds when $\mathbf c_y=0$. Nodes with trivial force therefore
couple trivially through this edge transport.

The normalization satisfies $\|\mathbf c_x\|^2<1$ for all $x$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:color-norm}).
Combined with $|\lambda_a|_\mathrm{op}\le 2$ for all Gell-Mann generators,
this gives the operator bound
\[
\|\Theta_e\|_{\mathrm{op}}
\le \frac{g_3}{2}\sum_{a=1}^8 |\Re(\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y)|\,\|\lambda_a\|_{\mathrm{op}}
\le \frac{g_3}{2}\cdot 8\cdot \|\mathbf c_x\|\,\|\mathbf c_y\|\cdot 2
< 8g_3,
\]
so the gauge coupling strength is controlled uniformly over all edges and runs.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Normalization of color vectors]
\label{lem:color-norm}
For every node $x\in\mcN$,
\[
\mathbf c_x^\dagger\mathbf c_x
=\frac{\tilde{\mathbf c}_x^\dagger\tilde{\mathbf c}_x}{\tilde{\mathbf c}_x^\dagger\tilde{\mathbf c}_x+\epsilon_c^2}\in[0,1),
\]
so $\mathbf c_x$ is well-defined and $\|\mathbf c_x\|<1$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Since $\epsilon_c>0$, the denominator $\tilde{\mathbf c}_x^\dagger\tilde{\mathbf c}_x+\epsilon_c^2$
is strictly positive.
The ratio $r/(r+\epsilon_c^2)$ for $r=\tilde{\mathbf c}_x^\dagger\tilde{\mathbf c}_x\ge0$ lies in $[0,1)$,
with the upper bound strict because $\epsilon_c^2>0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Color reparametrization]
\label{lem:color-reparam}
Let $\Omega:\mcN\to SU(3)$ be an arbitrary map.
Define $\mathbf c_x^\Omega:=\Omega(x)\mathbf c_x$ for each $x\in\mcN$.
Then $(\mathbf c_x^\Omega)^\dagger\mathbf c_x^\Omega=\mathbf c_x^\dagger\mathbf c_x$
and $\|\mathbf c_x^\Omega\|=\|\mathbf c_x\|$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Since $\Omega(x)\in U(3)$, $\Omega(x)^\dagger\Omega(x)=I$.
Hence
\[
(\mathbf c_x^\Omega)^\dagger\mathbf c_x^\Omega
=\mathbf c_x^\dagger\Omega(x)^\dagger\Omega(x)\mathbf c_x=\mathbf c_x^\dagger\mathbf c_x.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Gell-Mann generators]
\label{def:gellmann}
The eight Gell-Mann matrices $\lambda_a\in\C^{3\times3}$, $a=1,\dots,8$, are
\[
\lambda_1=\begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\\1&0&0\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix},\;
\lambda_2=\begin{pmatrix}0&-i&0\\ i&0&0\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix},\;
\lambda_3=\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&-1&0\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix},
\]
\[
\lambda_4=\begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\\0&0&0\\1&0&0\end{pmatrix},\;
\lambda_5=\begin{pmatrix}0&0&-i\\0&0&0\\ i&0&0\end{pmatrix},\;
\lambda_6=\begin{pmatrix}0&0&0\\0&0&1\\0&1&0\end{pmatrix},
\]
\[
\lambda_7=\begin{pmatrix}0&0&0\\0&0&-i\\0&i&0\end{pmatrix},\;
\lambda_8=\frac1{\sqrt3}\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&-2\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Each $\lambda_a$ is Hermitian and $\operatorname{tr}\lambda_a=0$.
The generators $\{i\lambda_a/2\}_{a=1}^8$ form a basis of the Lie algebra
$\mathfrak{su}(3)$ of traceless skew-Hermitian matrices~\cite{gellmann1962symmetries}.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Gell-Mann orthogonality]
\label{lem:gellmann}
The Gell-Mann matrices satisfy
\[
\tr(\lambda_a\lambda_b)=2\delta_{ab},\qquad a,b\in\{1,\dots,8\}.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Direct matrix multiplication: for $a=b$ each diagonal entry of $\lambda_a^2$
contributes, giving trace $2$; for $a\ne b$ the off-diagonal structure ensures
the trace vanishes.
This is a finite componentwise check whose result is well-known~\cite{gellmann1962symmetries}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Hermitian traceless span]
\label{lem:hermitian-span}
If $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_8\in\R$, then $H:=\sum_{a=1}^8\alpha_a\lambda_a$
is Hermitian and traceless.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Each $\lambda_a$ is Hermitian and traceless by Definition~\ref{def:gellmann}.
Real linear combinations preserve both properties.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Generator and transport on edges]
\label{def:transport}
For each oriented edge $e=(x\to y)\in\mcE$ define the algebra element
\[
\mathcal A_e:=\frac12\sum_{a=1}^8 \Re\!\bigl(\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y\bigr)\lambda_a\in\{H\in\C^{3\times3}:H^\dagger=H,\ \tr H=0\},
\qquad
\Theta_e:=g_3\,\mathcal A_e.
\]
and the $SU(3)$ edge transport
\[
U_3(e):=\exp(i\Theta_e)\in SU(3),
\qquad U_3(e^{-1}):=U_3(e)^{-1}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Justification of the transport construction]
\label{rem:transport-justification}
The formula
\[
\mathcal A_e=\frac12\sum_a\Re\!\bigl(\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y\bigr)\lambda_a
\]
is the orthogonal projection of the color bilinear $\mathbf c_x\mathbf c_y^\dagger$
onto the Hermitian traceless subspace spanned by $\{\lambda_a\}_{a=1}^8$.
To see this, recall that $\mathfrak{su}(3)$ carries the Killing-normalized inner
product $\langle A,B\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\tr(AB)$, under which $\{\lambda_a\}$ is
an orthogonal basis with $\langle\lambda_a,\lambda_b\rangle=\delta_{ab}$ by
Lemma~\ref{lem:gellmann}.
The Hermitian part of any matrix $M$ is $(M+M^\dagger)/2$.
The $a$-th component of the projection of $\mathbf c_x\mathbf c_y^\dagger$ is
\[
\bigl\langle\lambda_a,\tfrac12(\mathbf c_x\mathbf c_y^\dagger+\mathbf c_y\mathbf c_x^\dagger)\bigr\rangle
=\frac12\tr\!\bigl(\lambda_a(\mathbf c_x\mathbf c_y^\dagger+\mathbf c_y\mathbf c_x^\dagger)\bigr)
=\Re\!\bigl(\mathbf c_y^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_x\bigr)
=\Re\!\bigl(\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y\bigr)^*,
\]
where the last equality uses the Hermitian property $\lambda_a^\dagger=\lambda_a$.
Since the resulting coefficients are real, taking $\Re(\cdot)$ is consistent,
and $\mathcal A_e$ is exactly this projection. The coupling is $\Theta_e=g_3\mathcal A_e$.

The factor $g_3/2$ is chosen to match the continuum covariant derivative
$D_\mu=\partial_\mu-ig_3 A_\mu^a\lambda_a/2$:
in the weak-coupling limit $g_3\to0$ the transport linearizes to
\[
U_3(e)=I+i\Theta_e+O(g_3^2)
=I+ig_3\,\mathcal A_e+O(g_3^2)
=I+\frac{ig_3}{2}\sum_a\Re(\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y)\lambda_a+O(g_3^2),
\]
which is the first-order approximation to a continuum parallel transporter
$P\exp(ig_3\int A_\mu\,dx^\mu)$~\cite{rothe2012lattice}.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Edge transport is $SU(3)$]
\label{prop:su3-edge}
For every $e\in\mcE$, $U_3(e)\in SU(3)$ and $U_3(e^{-1})=U_3(e)^{-1}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each coefficient $\Re(\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y)$ is real.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:hermitian-span}, $\Theta_e$ is Hermitian and traceless, so
$i\Theta_e$ is skew-Hermitian and traceless.
Hence $U_3(e)=\exp(i\Theta_e)$ is unitary.
Since $\tr\Theta_e=0$, the identity $\det\exp(M)=\exp(\tr M)$ gives
$\det U_3(e)=\exp(i\tr\Theta_e)=1$.
Therefore $U_3(e)\in SU(3)$.
The inverse relation $U_3(e^{-1})=U_3(e)^{-1}$ is imposed by convention.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
There are no oriented self-loops in $\mcE$: all edges are either time-shifted,
same-time or influence-ahead, so source and target are distinct by definition.
If one formally assigns the transport formula to a self-loop, it still lands in
$SU(3)$ because $\mathcal A_e$ is Hermitian and traceless.
\end{remark}

\section{Holonomy and gauge invariance}
\label{sec:holonomy}

\begin{definition}[Path holonomy]
\label{def:path}
For an oriented path
$\gamma=(v_0\to v_1,\dots,v_{m-1}\to v_m)$ with $v_k\in\mcN$,
define the ordered product (left-to-right composition)
\[
U_3(\gamma):=U_3(v_0\to v_1)\,U_3(v_1\to v_2)\cdots U_3(v_{m-1}\to v_m)
=\prod_{k=1}^m U_3(v_{k-1}\to v_k).
\]
This ordering convention matches the standard lattice QCD convention in which
$U_\mu(x)$ transports from site $x$ to site $x+\hat\mu$~\cite{rothe2012lattice}.
If $v_m=v_0$, $\gamma$ is a loop and we define the Wilson loop trace
$W_3(\gamma):=\tr U_3(\gamma)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Triangle and plaquette holonomy]
\label{def:holonomy}
For a triangle or plaquette with oriented boundary edges, define
\[
U_3(\triangle):=\prod_{e\in\partial\triangle}U_3(e),\qquad
U_3(P):=\prod_{e\in\partial P}U_3(e),
\]
with factors ordered according to the oriented boundary sequence.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Plaquette factorization]
\label{prop:plaquette-factorization}
For each plaquette $P_{ij,t}$,
\[
U_3(P_{ij,t})=U_3(\triangle_{ij,t})\,U_3(\triangle_{ji,t}).
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The boundary of $P_{ij,t}$ is by definition $\partial P_{ij,t}=\partial\triangle_{ij,t}+\partial\triangle_{ji,t}$,
with the IG edges appearing with opposite orientation.
Specifically, $\partial\triangle_{ij,t}$ contains the edge $e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t})$ with coefficient $-1$,
meaning the holonomy for $\triangle_{ij,t}$ includes the factor $U_3(e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}))^{-1}$.
Similarly, $\partial\triangle_{ji,t}$ contains $e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{j,t}\to n_{i,t})$ with coefficient $-1$,
giving the factor $U_3(e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{j,t}\to n_{i,t}))^{-1}=U_3(e_{\mathrm{IG}}(n_{i,t}\to n_{j,t}))$.
The product of these two contributions is $U_3(e)^{-1}\cdot U_3(e)=I$,
so the IG edge cancels and $U_3(P_{ij,t})=U_3(\triangle_{ij,t})\,U_3(\triangle_{ji,t})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Gauge map]
\label{def:gauge}
A gauge map is any map $\Omega:\mcN\to SU(3)$.
For an edge $e=(x\to y)\in\mcE$ define the gauge-transformed transport
\[
U_3^\Omega(e):=\Omega(y)\,U_3(e)\,\Omega(x)^{-1}.
\]
Transformed color vectors are $\mathbf c_x^\Omega:=\Omega(x)\mathbf c_x$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Gauge invariance of Wilson traces]
\label{prop:gauge-invariance}
For any closed loop $\gamma=(v_0,\dots,v_m=v_0)$ and any gauge map $\Omega$,
\[
\tr\left(U_3^\Omega(\gamma)\right)=\tr\left(U_3(\gamma)\right).
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Compute the gauge-transformed holonomy:
\[
U_3^\Omega(\gamma)
=\prod_{k=1}^m \Omega(v_k)\,U_3(v_{k-1}\to v_k)\,\Omega(v_{k-1})^{-1}.
\]
Consecutive factors telescope: $\Omega(v_k)$ from step $k$ cancels
$\Omega(v_k)^{-1}$ from step $k+1$, since $v_k$ appears as the target of step $k$
and the source of step $k+1$.
After all $m$ steps, the remaining factors are $\Omega(v_m)=\Omega(v_0)$ on the left
and $\Omega(v_0)^{-1}$ on the right, giving
\[
U_3^\Omega(\gamma)=\Omega(v_0)\,U_3(\gamma)\,\Omega(v_0)^{-1}.
\]
Trace cyclicity then gives
$\tr(U_3^\Omega(\gamma))=\tr(\Omega(v_0)^{-1}\Omega(v_0)\,U_3(\gamma))=\tr(U_3(\gamma))$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Wilson action]
\label{def:action}
Let
\[
\mathcal P_\square:=\{P_{ij,t}\mid (i,j),(j,i)\in\mathcal P_t,\ t=0,\dots,T-1\}
\]
be the set of all plaquettes, and set $\beta:=6/g_3^2$.
The Wilson plaquette action is
\[
S_{\mathrm W}:=\beta\sum_{P\in\mathcal P_\square}\left(1-\frac13\Re\tr U_3(P)\right).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The normalization $1-\frac{1}{3}\Re\tr U_3(P)$ vanishes when $U_3(P)=I$ and is
non-negative for all $U_3(P)\in SU(3)$, since
$|\tr U|/3\le1$ by the triangle inequality applied to the three eigenvalues of $U$.
In the weak-coupling limit $g_3\to0$,
$U_3(P)\approx I+ig_3^2 F_{P}+O(g_3^4)$ for a field-strength matrix $F_P$,
and the action approaches $\frac{g_3^2}{6}\cdot 6/g_3^2\tr(F_P^2)/2=\frac12\tr(F_P^2)$,
recovering the continuum Yang-Mills action~\cite{wilson1974confinement,creutz1983quarks}.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Gauge invariance of Wilson action]
\label{prop:wilson-gauge}
For any gauge map $\Omega$, $S_{\mathrm W}^\Omega=S_{\mathrm W}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each plaquette trace $\Re\tr U_3(P)$ is invariant under gauge transformation by
Proposition~\ref{prop:gauge-invariance}.
The sum and the constant term are unchanged, so $S_{\mathrm W}^\Omega=S_{\mathrm W}$.
\end{proof}

\section{Matter sector on the same complex}
\label{sec:matter}

\begin{definition}[Matter fields and actions]
\label{def:matter}
For each node $x\in\mcN$ choose a fermionic spinor $\psi_x\in\C^3$,
its conjugate $\bar\psi_x\in(\C^3)^\dagger$, and a real scalar $\phi_x\in\R$.
Fix $m_F>0,\ m_\phi>0$, and a local potential $V_\phi:\R\to\R$.
For $e=(x\to y)\in\mcE$ set $\ell_{xy}:=\|\mathbf x(y)-\mathbf x(x)\|+\eta$ with $\eta>0$.
The covariant fermionic action is
\[
S_{\mathrm F}:=\sum_{e\in E_{\mathrm{CST}}\cup E_{\mathrm{IA}}}\bar\psi_{t(e)}\left(\psi_{t(e)}-U_3(e)\psi_{s(e)}\right)
+m_F\sum_{x\in\mcN}\bar\psi_x\psi_x,
\]
and the covariant scalar action is
\[
S_\phi:=\frac12\sum_{e\in E_{\mathrm{CST}}\cup E_{\mathrm{IG}}}\frac{(\phi_{t(e)}-\phi_{s(e)})^2}{\ell_{xy}}
+\sum_{x\in\mcN}\Bigl(\frac{m_\phi^2}{2}\phi_x^2+V_\phi(\phi_x)\Bigr).
\]
The gauge group acts on matter fields by
\[
\psi_x\mapsto\Omega(x)\psi_x,\qquad
\bar\psi_x\mapsto\bar\psi_x\Omega(x)^{-1},\qquad
\phi_x\mapsto\phi_x.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Distance weighting in the scalar action]
\label{rem:distance-weight}
The factor $1/\ell_{xy}$ in the scalar kinetic term is a discrete analogue of
the continuum normalization $(\nabla_\mu\phi)^2$.
In the continuum, the square of a derivative has dimensions $[\phi]^2/[\text{length}]^2$,
so a finite difference $(\phi_y-\phi_x)^2$ must be divided by $\|\mathbf x(y)-\mathbf x(x)\|$
(one power of length rather than two) for a first-order finite-difference scheme.
Specifically, if $\phi_{t(e)}-\phi_{s(e)}\approx (\nabla\phi)\cdot(\mathbf x(y)-\mathbf x(x))$
with $|\mathbf x(y)-\mathbf x(x)|\approx\ell_{xy}$, then
$(\phi_{t(e)}-\phi_{s(e)})^2/\ell_{xy}\approx \ell_{xy}\,|\nabla\phi|^2$,
and summing over a regular lattice with spacing $a=\ell_{xy}$ recovers
$a^d\,|\nabla\phi|^2$ as in the standard scalar lattice action~\cite{montvay1994quantum}.

The parameter $\eta>0$ prevents division by zero for coincident node positions
(self-loops, or walkers occupying the same point at the same time).
In practice $\eta\ll\ell_{\mathrm{visc}}$ so that $\ell_{xy}\approx\|\mathbf x(y)-\mathbf x(x)\|$
for all non-coincident edges.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Matter gauge invariance]
\label{prop:matter-gauge}
Under the gauge action of Definition~\ref{def:matter},
\[
S_{\mathrm F}^\Omega=S_{\mathrm F},\qquad S_\phi^\Omega=S_\phi.
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
For each fermionic edge term,
\begin{align*}
\bar\psi_{t(e)}\!\left(\psi_{t(e)}-U_3(e)\psi_{s(e)}\right)
&\mapsto
\bar\psi_{t(e)}\Omega(t(e))^{-1}
\!\left(\Omega(t(e))\psi_{t(e)}-\Omega(t(e))U_3(e)\Omega(s(e))^{-1}\Omega(s(e))\psi_{s(e)}\right)\\
&=\bar\psi_{t(e)}\!\left(\psi_{t(e)}-U_3(e)\psi_{s(e)}\right),
\end{align*}
where we used $\Omega(t(e))^{-1}\Omega(t(e))=I$ and $\Omega(s(e))^{-1}\Omega(s(e))=I$.
The mass term $m_F\bar\psi_x\psi_x$ is invariant because $\Omega(x)^{-1}\Omega(x)=I$ pointwise.
The scalar action $S_\phi$ depends only on scalar fields, which are gauge-invariant,
so $S_\phi^\Omega=S_\phi$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Edge-type selection in the matter actions]
\label{rem:edge-selection}
The fermionic action uses $E_{\mathrm{CST}}\cup E_{\mathrm{IA}}$ rather than
the full edge set $\mcE$.
CST edges encode the temporal forward propagation of each walker: a fermion at
node $n_{i,t}$ propagates causally to $n_{i,t+1}$.
IA edges encode the causal influence of a companion $j$ at time $t$ on walker $i$
at the later time $t+1$: this is precisely the interaction-ahead coupling.
Together, $E_{\mathrm{CST}}\cup E_{\mathrm{IA}}$ spans all edges that connect
a node to a causally later node, which is the natural support for a
first-order discrete Dirac operator.

The scalar action uses $E_{\mathrm{CST}}\cup E_{\mathrm{IG}}$ instead.
Scalars have no preferred propagation direction and can couple
instantaneously within a timestep; the IG edges at the same time~$t$ provide
exactly this same-time stiffness.
Using IA edges in the scalar action would couple $\phi_{n_{i,t+1}}$ to
$\phi_{n_{j,t}}$ across a time boundary, which is less natural for a scalar
and would break the symmetry between $i$ and $j$ at time~$t$.
\end{remark}

\section{Finite lattice-QFT data package}
\label{sec:package}

\begin{theorem}[Finite $SU(3)$ data package]
\label{thm:package}
Given a run $\mathscr R$ as in Definition~\ref{def:run} and the constructions
in Sections~\ref{sec:run}--\ref{sec:matter}, the tuple
\[
\mathcal Q=
\Bigl(
T,\{\mathcal A(t)\},\{\mathcal P_t\},\mcN,\mcE,\mcT,
\{x_i(t),v_i(t),\Phi_i(t)\}_{i,t},K_\rho,\nu_{\mathrm{visc}},
\hbar_{\mathrm{eff}},\mu_p,\ell_0,\epsilon_c,g_3,\beta,\eta,
\{U_3(e)\}_{e\in\mcE},S_{\mathrm W},S_{\mathrm F},S_\phi
\Bigr)
\]
is finite and explicit.
It carries:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item a directed 2-complex $(\mcN,\mcE,\mcT)$ with boundary operators
      $(\partial_1,\partial_2)$ satisfying $\partial_1\partial_2=0$;
\item $SU(3)$ parallel transport maps $\{U_3(e)\}_{e\in\mcE}$;
\item gauge-invariant Wilson traces and Wilson action $S_{\mathrm W}$;
\item gauge-covariant fermionic and scalar matter actions $S_{\mathrm F}$, $S_\phi$.
\end{enumerate}
All objects required for a subsequent axiom-verification paper are present.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Finiteness of $(\mcN,\mcE,\mcT)$ is Proposition~\ref{prop:finiteness}.
Finiteness of $\{U_3(e)\}$ follows because $\mcE$ is finite and each $U_3(e)$
is defined by a finite matrix exponential.
The actions $S_{\mathrm W}$, $S_{\mathrm F}$, $S_\phi$ are finite sums over
finite index sets by the same finiteness.
Gauge invariance of $S_{\mathrm W}$ is Proposition~\ref{prop:wilson-gauge};
gauge invariance of $S_{\mathrm F}$ and $S_\phi$ is
Proposition~\ref{prop:matter-gauge}.
The boundary identity $\partial_1\partial_2=0$ is Proposition~\ref{prop:complex}.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Relationship to standard lattice QCD}

Wilson's original lattice gauge theory~\cite{wilson1974confinement}
assigns an independent $SU(3)$-valued link variable $U_\mu(x)$ to each
directed bond of a regular hypercubic spacetime lattice.
The link variables are the fundamental integration variables of the path integral,
and the gauge group is imposed as an exact symmetry from the outset.
The present construction differs in two respects.

First, the underlying complex $(\mcN,\mcE,\mcT)$ is not a regular lattice but an
irregular, data-dependent graph whose topology changes from run to run.
The node set is a finite subset of a spacetime determined by the alive walkers,
and the edge set encodes three physically distinct types of coupling:
temporal propagation (CST), same-time interaction (IG), and causal interaction-ahead (IA).
In lattice QCD all bonds are topologically equivalent; here they are not.

Second, the link variables $U_3(e)$ are not independent: they are
\emph{derived} from the trajectory data via the color construction of
Definitions~\ref{def:colors}--\ref{def:transport}.
The gauge group, the coupling strength $g_3$, and the parallel transport
all emerge from the viscous dynamics rather than being postulated.
This derivation-vs-postulation distinction is the central new feature of the
construction.

Despite these differences, the Wilson action
$S_{\mathrm W}=\beta\sum_P(1-\frac{1}{3}\Re\tr U_3(P))$
has exactly the standard lattice QCD form~\cite{creutz1983quarks,rothe2012lattice},
and all the gauge-theoretic machinery (holonomies, Wilson loops, covariant
derivatives) is identical to the standard treatment.
This means that any lattice QCD observable defined in terms of the link variables
can be computed on $\mathcal Q$ without modification.

\subsection{Continuum limit}

In standard lattice QCD one takes the continuum limit by sending the lattice
spacing $a\to0$ while tuning $g$ to keep physical masses fixed (asymptotic
freedom).
An analogous program for the interaction complex would require:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item a sequence of runs with increasing $T$ and $N$ such that the empirical
      spatial density $N/V$ diverges and the time step $\Delta t\to0$;
\item a rescaling of the coupling $g_3\sim a(\log a)^{-1}$ and the de~Broglie
      parameters with the effective lattice spacing $a\sim\ell_{\mathrm{visc}}/N^{1/3}$;
\item convergence of the empirical measure $\frac{1}{N}\sum_i\delta_{x_i(t)}$
      to a smooth spatial density as $N\to\infty$, as supplied by the kinetic
      convergence program in the companion paper.
\end{enumerate}
This program requires results from the kinetic convergence theory developed in
the companion paper and is outside the present scope.

\subsection{Quantization and partition function}

The data package $\mathcal Q$ provides a well-defined action
$S=S_{\mathrm W}+S_{\mathrm F}+S_\phi$
on the finite-dimensional field variables
$\{\psi_x,\bar\psi_x,\phi_x\}_{x\in\mcN}$.
The path integral over these fields,
\[
Z=\int \Bigl[\prod_{x\in\mcN}d\psi_x\,d\bar\psi_x\,d\phi_x\Bigr]
\exp\!\bigl(-S_{\mathrm W}-S_{\mathrm F}-S_\phi\bigr),
\]
is a finite-dimensional ordinary integral over
the gauge sector $SU(3)^{|\mcN|}$, finite-dimensional fermionic Grassmann variables,
and the scalar sector $\R^{|\mcN|}$.
Gauge-fixing via Faddeev--Popov is straightforward in the finite setting~\cite{montvay1994quantum}.
The resulting $Z$ is well-defined without any ultraviolet regularization;
all divergences that would appear in a continuum QFT are absent because the
complex is finite.
Evaluating $Z$ numerically for realistic run data and comparing with QCD predictions
is a natural next step.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We have constructed a complete, finite, and $SU(3)$ gauge-invariant lattice QFT
data package $\mathcal Q$ from the discrete trajectory data of a viscous
multi-particle system.
The construction proceeds in five conceptual steps:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item Build the directed 2-complex $(\mcN,\mcE,\mcT)$ from the spacetime nodes,
      causal edges, interaction edges, and interaction-ahead edges of the run.
\item Compute the row-normalized viscous force $\mathbf F^{\mathrm{visc}}(x)$
      at each node from the interaction graph weights.
\item Form complex color vectors $\mathbf c_x\in\C^3$ by modulating each force
      component with a de~Broglie kinematic phase and normalizing.
\item Define the $SU(3)$ edge transport $U_3(e)=\exp(i\Theta_e)$ by projecting
      the bilinear color overlap $\mathbf c_x^\dagger\lambda_a\mathbf c_y$ onto
      the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{su}(3)$ via the Gell-Mann basis.
\item Construct the Wilson plaquette action $S_{\mathrm W}$ and the covariant
      matter actions $S_{\mathrm F}$, $S_\phi$ on the same complex.
\end{enumerate}
All objects are explicit and finite (Theorem~\ref{thm:package}).
The gauge group $SU(3)$ is not imposed but emerges from the three-dimensional
viscous dynamics together with the Gell-Mann projection.

Three major extensions are deferred to companion papers:
(i) the formal continuum limit as run data becomes dense;
(ii) path-integral quantization and numerical evaluation of $Z$;
(iii) axiom verification --- checking that $\mathcal Q$ satisfies the
Osterwalder-Schrader axioms adapted to the irregular complex.
