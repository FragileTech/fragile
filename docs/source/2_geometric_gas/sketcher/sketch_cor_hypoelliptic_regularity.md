## I. Theorem Statement

:::{prf:corollary} Hypoelliptic Regularity
:label: cor-hypoelliptic-regularity

If $\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}[\rho] = f$ with $f \in C^\infty(\Omega)$, then $\rho \in C^\infty(\Omega)$.

In particular, if $\mathcal{L}[\rho_\infty] = 0$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{jump}}[\rho_\infty] \in C^\infty$, then $\rho_\infty \in C^\infty(\Omega)$.
:::

**Informal Restatement**:

The corollary states two related results about regularity of solutions to kinetic equations:

1. **General Statement**: If the kinetic operator $\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}$ applied to a density $\rho$ equals a smooth function $f$, then the density $\rho$ itself must be smooth (infinitely differentiable).

2. **QSD Application**: For the quasi-stationary distribution $\rho_\infty$, if the stationarity equation holds ($\mathcal{L}[\rho_\infty] = 0$) and the jump operator produces a smooth result ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{jump}}[\rho_\infty] \in C^\infty$), then the QSD is smooth.

The key insight is that even though the kinetic operator has **degenerate diffusion** (noise only in velocity, not position), the coupling between position and velocity through the drift term allows smoothness to propagate from the right-hand side to the solution. This is the essence of **hypoelliptic regularity**.

## IV. Detailed Proof Sketch

### Overview

The proof exploits the fundamental property of **hypoelliptic operators**: even though diffusion is degenerate (only in velocity, not position), the coupling through the drift term allows regularity to propagate. The key is Hörmander's bracket condition, which says that the vector fields $\{X_0, X_j\}$ generate motion in all directions through their commutators.

The proof has two parts mirroring the corollary's statement:
1. **General regularity**: Show that any solution to $\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}[\rho] = f$ with $f \in C^\infty$ must satisfy $\rho \in C^\infty$
2. **QSD application**: Apply this to the stationarity equation to conclude $\rho_\infty \in C^\infty$

The argument is essentially a **citation of Hörmander's theorem** combined with **verification that its hypotheses are met** via the already-proven bracket condition.

### Proof Outline (Top-Level)

The proof proceeds in **4 main stages**:

1. **Hörmander Form**: Express $\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}$ in the form $\sum_{j=1}^d X_j^2 + X_0$ required by Hörmander's theorem
2. **Bracket Generation**: Verify the Lie algebra generated by $\{X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_d\}$ spans the tangent space (cite lem-hormander)
3. **Hypoellipticity Application**: Apply Hörmander's theorem to conclude $\rho \in C^\infty$ when $f \in C^\infty$
4. **QSD Specialization**: Substitute the stationarity equation to obtain the "in particular" clause

## V. Technical Deep Dives

### Challenge 1: Coefficient Regularity for Hörmander's Theorem

**Why Difficult**:

Hörmander's theorem in its classical formulation requires the vector fields $X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_d$ to have $C^\infty$ coefficients. For the kinetic operator, this translates to requiring the potential $U$ to be $C^\infty$.

However, **Assumption A1** in the geometric gas framework states:

> **A1 (Confinement)**: The potential $U: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies certain growth conditions to ensure confinement...

The assumption may specify only that $U \in C^2$ or $C^k$ for some finite $k$ (needed for well-posedness of the SDE), not necessarily $U \in C^\infty$.

If $U \in C^k$ for finite $k$, then:
- The drift vector field $X_0$ has $C^{k-1}$ coefficients (due to $\nabla_x U$)
- Hörmander's theorem in its sharpest form may not apply
- We can only conclude $\rho \in C^{k}$ or use **subelliptic estimates** to get $C^{k,\alpha}$ regularity

**Proposed Solution**:

**Option 1: Strengthen Assumption A1**
- Explicitly require $U \in C^\infty(\mathcal{X})$ in Assumption A1
- This is physically reasonable for many potentials (e.g., polynomial, exponential, Gaussian)
- **Advantage**: Clean application of Hörmander's theorem
- **Disadvantage**: Restricts generality

**Option 2: Use Hypoelliptic Schauder Estimates**
- Apply **subelliptic estimates** (Bony 1969, Rothschild-Stein 1976) which work with $C^{k,\alpha}$ coefficients
- Conclusion becomes: if $U \in C^{k,\alpha}$ and $f \in C^{m,\beta}$, then $\rho \in C^{m+2s,\gamma}$ for some subelliptic exponent $s < 1$
- Iterate to gain regularity up to the smoothness of the coefficients
- **Advantage**: More general, works with lower regularity potentials
- **Disadvantage**: Technically heavier, requires subelliptic calculus

**Option 3: Bootstrap Approach**
- Start with $\rho \in H^1_w$ (Sobolev regularity from thm-uniqueness-hypoelliptic-regularity)
- Use local $C^{k,\alpha}$ estimates to gain regularity step-by-step
- Stop when regularity saturates at the coefficient smoothness level
- **Advantage**: Constructive, shows explicit regularity gain
- **Disadvantage**: More work, may not reach $C^\infty$ without $U \in C^\infty$

**Recommendation**:

For the corollary as stated ($\rho \in C^\infty$), we should:
1. **Verify** whether Assumption A1 implicitly or explicitly guarantees $U \in C^\infty$
2. If not, **add a footnote** stating: "The conclusion $\rho \in C^\infty$ holds if $U \in C^\infty$; for $U \in C^{k,\alpha}$, we obtain $\rho \in C^{k,\alpha}$ via subelliptic estimates."
3. For **thm-qsd-smoothness**, explicitly assume $U \in C^\infty$ (or $\kappa_{\text{kill}} \in C^\infty$ as already stated, and $U \in C^\infty$)

**Alternative if Fails**:

If $U$ is only $C^2$, restate the corollary with appropriate regularity class (e.g., $\rho \in C^{2,\alpha}$) and cite subelliptic estimates.

### Challenge 2: Forward vs Backward Operator and Adjoint Hypoellipticity

**Why Difficult**:

In PDE theory, there's a distinction between:
- **Backward operator** $L$: Acts on test functions $\phi$, appears in weak formulations $\int \phi \, L^* \rho$
- **Forward operator** $L^*$ (adjoint): Acts on densities $\rho$, appears in the Fokker-Planck equation $\partial_t \rho = L^* \rho$

Hörmander's theorem is typically stated for the backward operator: if $L$ is hypoelliptic and $L\phi = g$ with $g \in C^\infty$, then $\phi \in C^\infty$.

However, the corollary applies to the **forward operator** $\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}$ acting on the density $\rho$:
$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}[\rho] = f
$$

The question is: does hypoellipticity transfer from $L$ to $L^*$?

**Proposed Technique**:

**Yes**, hypoellipticity is **stable under adjoints**. This is a standard result:

> **Theorem (Hypoellipticity of Adjoint)**: If $L$ is a hypoelliptic operator, then its formal adjoint $L^*$ is also hypoelliptic.

**Justification**:
1. The bracket condition depends only on the **principal symbol** of the operator (the second-order terms)
2. The principal symbol of $L$ and $L^*$ are related by complex conjugation/transposition
3. The bracket structure is preserved under this transformation
4. Therefore, if $\{X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_d\}$ generate the tangent space for $L$, the corresponding vector fields for $L^*$ also generate the tangent space

**Reference**:
- Hörmander, "Hypoelliptic second order differential equations," Acta Math. 119 (1967), Section 1.4
- Trèves, "Introduction to Pseudodifferential and Fourier Integral Operators," Volume 1, Chapter 6

**Application to our case**:
- The bracket computation in **lem-uniqueness-hormander-verification** can be done for either the forward or backward operator (the commutators $[X_0, X_j]$ are the same)
- Therefore, both $\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}$ (forward) and its adjoint (backward) are hypoelliptic
- The corollary applies directly to the forward operator

**Resolution**:

This is **not a real obstacle**, just a clarification needed in the exposition. The proof should mention:

### Challenge 3: Boundary Regularity and Interior vs Global Smoothness

**Why Difficult**:

Hörmander's theorem provides **interior regularity**: if $Lu = f$ with $f \in C^\infty(\Omega)$, then $u \in C^\infty_{\text{loc}}(\Omega)$ (smooth in the interior of $\Omega$).

However, the domain $\Omega = \mathcal{X} \times \mathbb{R}^d$ may have a boundary:
- If $\mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$ is a bounded domain, then $\partial\Omega$ includes $\partial \mathcal{X} \times \mathbb{R}^d$
- The behavior of $\rho$ near $\partial \mathcal{X}$ depends on boundary conditions (reflecting, absorbing, killing, etc.)

**Questions**:
1. Does the corollary claim $\rho \in C^\infty(\Omega)$ (smooth up to the boundary) or $\rho \in C^\infty_{\text{loc}}(\Omega)$ (smooth in the interior)?
2. If boundary regularity is needed, what additional conditions are required?

**Proposed Technique**:

**Approach 1: Interior Regularity Interpretation** (Simplest)
- Interpret the corollary as providing **interior smoothness**: $\rho \in C^\infty(\text{int}(\Omega))$
- This is the direct consequence of Hörmander's theorem
- **Advantage**: No additional assumptions needed
- **Disadvantage**: Doesn't address boundary behavior

**Approach 2: Boundary-Compatible Conditions**
- For smoothness up to the boundary, need **subelliptic boundary conditions**
- This means the boundary conditions (e.g., Neumann, Dirichlet, oblique derivative) must be compatible with the hypoelliptic structure
- References:
  - Hérau-Nier, "Isotropic hypoellipticity and trend to equilibrium for the Fokker-Planck equation with a high-degree potential," Arch. Ration. Mech. Anal. 171 (2004)
  - Lebeau, "Hypoelliptic second order differential equations with subelliptic boundary conditions," Proc. ICM (2006)
- For the geometric gas, the **reflecting boundary conditions** on $\partial \mathcal{X}$ are typically compatible
- **Advantage**: Provides global regularity
- **Disadvantage**: Requires verifying boundary compatibility (technical)

**Approach 3: Velocity Space is Unbounded**
- Note that the velocity space is $\mathbb{R}^d$ (unbounded), not a compact set
- The "boundary" in $\Omega$ is only in the position component: $\partial \mathcal{X} \times \mathbb{R}^d$
- Regularity in the velocity direction is automatically global (no boundary issues)
- Only need to handle regularity near $\partial \mathcal{X}$
- For many applications, the QSD has **exponential decay** in velocity, so boundary effects at infinity are negligible

**Recommendation**:

For the corollary statement:
1. **Primary interpretation**: Interior $C^\infty$ regularity (standard for Hörmander)
2. **Footnote or remark**: "For regularity up to the boundary $\partial \mathcal{X}$, the boundary conditions must be compatible with the hypoelliptic structure. For the geometric gas with reflecting boundaries, this compatibility holds (see Hérau-Nier 2004)."
3. In **thm-qsd-smoothness**, this is less critical since the QSD typically has **compact support in $x$** (bounded away from $\partial \mathcal{X}$) due to the killing mechanism near the boundary

**Alternative if Fails**:

If boundary regularity cannot be established, restrict the corollary to:
$$
\rho \in C^\infty(\text{int}(\Omega))
$$
This is still sufficient for most applications, as the QSD is primarily relevant in the interior of the alive set.

### Alternative 1: Bootstrap via Hypoelliptic Sobolev Regularity

**Approach**:

Instead of directly citing Hörmander's $C^\infty$ regularity, use **hypoelliptic Sobolev estimates** to build regularity iteratively:

1. Start with $\rho \in L^2_w(\Omega)$ (weighted $L^2$)
2. Apply **thm-uniqueness-hypoelliptic-regularity** (from 08_propagation_chaos.md):
   > If $-\mathcal{L}_{\text{lin}} u = f$ with $f \in L^2_w$, then $u \in H^1_w$ with bound $\|u\|_{H^1_w} \le C_{\text{hypo}} \|f\|_{L^2_w}$
3. Gain one Sobolev derivative: $\rho \in H^1_w$
4. Use **Sobolev embedding** (if available on $\Omega$) to get $\rho \in L^p$ for higher $p$
5. Iterate: apply regularity estimate again with improved integrability
6. Use **local elliptic regularity** in the velocity variable (where the operator is non-degenerate) to gain $C^{k,\alpha}$ estimates
7. Bootstrap to $C^\infty$ if coefficients allow

**Pros**:
- **Constructive**: Shows explicit regularity gain at each step
- **Quantitative**: Provides Sobolev norm bounds, not just qualitative smoothness
- **Robust**: Works even if coefficients have limited regularity (terminates at appropriate $C^k$ level)

**Cons**:
- **Technically heavier**: Requires setting up weighted Sobolev spaces, Sobolev embedding theorems
- **Less direct**: More steps than just citing Hörmander's theorem
- **May not reach $C^\infty$**: If coefficients are only $C^k$, bootstrap stops at $C^k$
- **Complexity**: Needs careful tracking of weights and constants

**When to Consider**:

This approach is valuable when:
- Coefficient regularity is limited (e.g., $U \in C^{2,\alpha}$ only)
- Quantitative bounds on derivatives are needed (not just existence of smoothness)
- Working in the context of **thm-qsd-smoothness** where the bootstrap is explicitly described
- Want to avoid citing the full strength of Hörmander's theorem

### Alternative 2: Semigroup/Malliavin Calculus Approach

**Approach**:

Use **probabilistic methods** to prove regularity:

1. Represent the solution via the **kinetic semigroup**: $\rho_t = P_t \rho_0$ where $P_t$ is the semigroup generated by $\mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}$
2. For $t > 0$, the semigroup has a **smooth density** (transition density $p_t(z, z')$) due to hypoellipticity
3. Show that $p_t(\cdot, z') \in C^\infty$ for all $z' \in \Omega$ and $t > 0$
4. If $\rho_\infty$ is a stationary distribution, it can be represented as:
   $$
   \rho_\infty = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T P_t \rho_0 \, dt
   $$
   (ergodic average)
5. Since each $P_t \rho_0$ is smooth for $t > 0$, the limit (if it exists) is also smooth
6. Alternatively, use **Malliavin calculus**: show the transition density satisfies an **integration-by-parts formula** (non-degeneracy of Malliavin covariance), which implies smoothness

**Pros**:
- **Probabilistic intuition**: Provides understanding via stochastic processes
- **Global in time**: Connects stationary regularity to dynamical properties
- **Robust**: Malliavin calculus works even for degenerate diffusions (fits hypoelliptic setting)
- **Alternative verification**: Malliavin non-degeneracy can be verified via bracket condition (same as Hörmander)

**Cons**:
- **More abstract**: Requires Malliavin calculus machinery (not yet introduced in the framework)
- **Stochastic analysis**: Shifts from PDE to probability theory (different technical framework)
- **Ergodic assumptions**: Needs to verify ergodicity and existence of ergodic limit
- **Overkill for a corollary**: This is a much heavier approach for what is essentially a direct consequence of Hörmander

**When to Consider**:

This approach is useful when:
- Working in a probabilistic setting (SDE formulation rather than PDE)
- Interested in **dynamical convergence** to the QSD (not just stationary regularity)
- Malliavin calculus is already part of the framework (not the case here)
- Want to establish **positive densities** in addition to smoothness (Malliavin + Hörmander together imply strict positivity)

**Reference**:
- Villani, "Hypocoercivity," Mem. Amer. Math. Soc. 202 (2009) - uses both PDE and probabilistic methods
- Nualart, "The Malliavin Calculus and Related Topics," Springer (2006) - Malliavin approach to regularity

### Alternative 3: Direct PDE Regularity Theory (Schauder Estimates)

**Approach**:

Use classical **elliptic/parabolic regularity theory** adapted to the hypoelliptic setting:

1. Write the equation as:
   $$
   \sigma^2 \Delta_v \rho + (\text{lower order terms}) = 2f
   $$
2. In the velocity variable $v$, the operator is **uniformly elliptic** (full diffusion)
3. Apply **Schauder estimates** in the $v$ direction: if $f \in C^{k,\alpha}$, then $\rho \in C^{k+2,\alpha}$ locally in $v$
4. Use the **coupling through drift** ($v \cdot \nabla_x$) to transfer regularity from $v$ to $x$
5. Iterate to gain regularity in both variables
6. This is essentially reproving Hörmander's theorem using Schauder's method (historical approach before Hörmander's general framework)

**Pros**:
- **Classical PDE**: Uses well-known Schauder estimates
- **Elementary**: Doesn't require Lie bracket machinery
- **Constructive**: Shows how regularity propagates between variables

**Cons**:
- **Very technical**: Requires careful tracking of regularity in mixed derivatives
- **Reinventing the wheel**: This is what Hörmander's theorem was designed to systematize
- **Limited generality**: Harder to extend to more general bracket conditions
- **Long and tedious**: Much longer proof than citing Hörmander

**When to Consider**:

This approach is mainly of **historical or pedagogical interest**. It's how regularity of kinetic operators was proven before Hörmander's general theorem. Not recommended unless:
- Teaching the development of hypoelliptic theory
- Want to avoid citing advanced theorems
- Need very explicit constants in the regularity estimates
